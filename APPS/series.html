Section 9.2 Infinite Series

We know how to add up a finite set of numbers \(a_1 + a_2 + \cdots + a_n\text{.}\) But what does it mean to add up an infinite sequence of numbers

\begin{equation*} a_1 + a_2 + \cdots + a_j + \cdots ? \end{equation*}

A good way to make sense of this is to form the new sequence \((s_n)\) where

\begin{equation*} s_n := a_1 + a_2 + \dots + a_n \end{equation*}

and to define the infinite sum

\begin{equation*} a_1 + a_2 + \cdots + a_j + \cdots = \sum_{j=1}^\infty a_j := \limn s_n \end{equation*}

to be the limit of the sequence \((s_n)\) whenever it exists. The numbers \(s_n\) are called the partial sums of \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty a_j\text{.}\) If the sequence of partial sums \((s_n)\) does not converge, we assign no meaning to \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty a_j\text{.}\) More formally:

Definition 9.2.1.

Given a sequence \((a_{j})\text{,}\) we say that the infinite series \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty a_{j}\) converges if the sequence of partial sums

\begin{equation*} s_{n}=\sum_{j=1}^{n}a_{j} \end{equation*}

converges as \(n\rightarrow\infty\text{.}\) When \((s_n)\) converges we denote its limit by \(\sum_{j=1}^{\infty}a_{j}\text{.}\)

Alternatively, given the sequence \((a_n)\text{,}\) we can equivalently define the sequence \((s_n)\) recursively by \(s_1 = a_1\) and \(s_{n} = a_n + s_{n-1}\) for \(n \geq 2\text{.}\)

Question: Give an example of an infinite series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) which converges, and an example of one which fails to converge.

Decimal expansions can be thought of as special cases of infinite series: given a sequence \((b_j)\) with each \(b_j \in \{0, 1, \dots , 9\}\text{,}\) we can interpret the decimal expansion \(0.b_1b_2 \dots\) as the infinite series \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty \frac{b_j}{10^j}\text{.}\)

A word of warning about a possible source of confusion. Given a sequence \((a_n)\text{,}\) we can consider two questions about it: convergence of the sequence \((a_n)\text{,}\) and convergence of the series \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty a_j\text{.}\) It is important to realise that these are two completely different things. Convergence of the sequence \((a_n)\) is just what it says, while convergence of the series \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty a_j\)is defined as convergence of the auxiliary sequence \((s_n)\text{.}\) So it's always important to be clear whether we are talking about convergence of the sequence \((a_n)\) or convergence of the series \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty a_j\text{.}\) Some texts use the word summable to describe convergent series, and this goes some way towards relieving any potential confusion, but the term convergent series is just too ingrained in mathematical language to be eradicated. (See also Exercise 9.4.6.)

Sometimes we will also work with series which start from a different position, like \(\sum_{j=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{j(j-1)}\text{,}\) and the definition is the same: we say this series is convergent if the partial sums \(\sum_{j=2}^{n} \frac{1}{j(j-1)}\) converge. Whether we use the dummy variable \(j\) or \(k\) (or anything else) doesn't matter: \(\sum_{j=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{j(j-1)}\) has exactly the same meaning as \(\sum_{k=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k(k-1)}\) or \(\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n(n-1)}\text{.}\)

Example 9.2.2.

The quintessential example of a convergent series is the geometric series. Recall that for any real number \(r \neq 1\text{,}\)

\begin{equation} \sum_{j=0}^{n} ar ^{j}=\frac{a(1-r^{n+1})}{1-r}\tag{9.2.1} \end{equation}

If \(|r|\lt 1\text{,}\) then \(|r^{n+1}|=|r|^{n+1}\rightarrow 0\) by Example 8.1.4, and so \(r^{n+1}\rightarrow 0\) as well. Thus,

\begin{equation*} \limn \sum_{j=0}^{n}ar^{j} = \limn \frac{a(1-r^{n+1})}{1-r} = \frac{\limn a(1-r^{n+1})}{1-r}=\frac{a- a\limn r^{n+1}}{1-r}=\frac{a}{1-r}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus, when \(|r|\lt 1\text{,}\) \(\sum_{j=0}^\infty a r^{j}\) is convergent, and \(\sum_{j=0}^{\infty} a r^{j}=\frac{a}{1-r}\text{.}\) We'll see below that this series converges if and only if \(|r|\lt 1\text{.}\)

One useful necessary condition for a series to converge is the following:

Proposition 9.2.3.

Suppose the series \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty a_j\) converges. Then the sequence \((a_j)\) satisfies \(a_j \to 0\text{.}\)

Proof.

Let \(s_n = \sum_{j=1}^n a_j\text{.}\) Then for some \(s \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) \(s_n \to s\text{.}\) Hence \(a_n = s_n - s_{n-1} \to s-s =0\text{.}\)

As a consequence of this we see that the geometric series of Example 9.2.2 diverges when \(|r| \geq 1\) since the sequence \((ar^j)_{j=1}^\infty\) does not converge to \(0\) when \(|r| \geq 1\text{.}\)

Just as we had rules for manipulating limits of sequences, we also have rules for manipulating infinite sums. The one which follows says that the operation of taking infinite sums is linear. Its proof will use the rules for limits of sequences.

Proposition 9.2.4.

Let \((a_{j})\) and \((b_{j})\) be sequences. If \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty a_{j}\) and \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty b_{j}\) are convergent, then so is \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty (a_{j}+b_{j})\text{,}\) and

\begin{equation*} \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} (a_{j}+b_{j})=\sum_{j=1}^{\infty} a_{j}+\sum_{j=1}^{\infty} b_{j}\text{.} \end{equation*}

If \(c\in\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) then \(\sum ca_{j}\) is convergent and

\begin{equation*} \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} ca_{j}=c \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} a_{j}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Proof.

We observe that

\begin{align*} \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} (a_{j}+b_{j}) \amp = \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \sum_{j=1}^{n}(a_{j}+b_{j}) \amp =\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \left(\sum_{j=1}^{n} a_{j}+\sum_{j=1}^{n}b_{j}\right) =\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \sum_{j=1}^{n} a_{j}+\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\sum_{j=1}^{n}b_{j}\\ \amp =\sum_{j=1}^{\infty} a_{j}+\sum_{j=1}^{\infty}b_{j}\text{.} \end{align*}

Similarly,

\begin{equation*} \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} ca_{j} =\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\sum_{j=1}^{n} ca_{j} =c\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\sum_{j=1}^{n} a_{j} =c\sum_{j=1}^{\infty} a_{j}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Problem 9.2.5.

What about a rule for products of series?

In Calculus and its Applications, you will learn about several tests for convergence of series. We will discuss only one here.

Theorem 9.2.6. Comparison Test.

If \(b_j \geq 0\) for all \(j\) and \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty b_{j}\) is convergent, and if \(|a_{j}|\leq b_{j}\) for all \(j\text{,}\) then \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty a_{j}\) is also convergent, and moreover \(|\sum_{j=1}^\infty a_j| \leq \sum_{j=1}^\infty b_j\text{.}\)

Proof.

The idea is to write \(a_j = (a_j - b_j) + b_j\) and to use Proposition 9.2.4. We know that \(\sum_j b_j\) converges by hypothesis, and if we knew that \(\sum_j(a_j - b_j)\) converged we would be in business.

Note first that, since \(a_j \leq b_j\) for all \(j\text{,}\) we have \(t_n \geq 0\) for all \(n\text{,}\) and moreover the sequence \((t_n)\) given by

\begin{equation*} t_{n}=\sum_{j=1}^{n}(b_{j}-a_{j}) \end{equation*}

is increasing. Next, note that \(b_{j}-a_{j}\leq 2b_{j}\text{,}\) and so

\begin{equation*} t_{n}\leq \sum_{j=1}^{n}2b_{j}\leq \sum_{j=1}^{\infty}2b_{j} \end{equation*}

and since this latter sum converges by Proposition 9.2.4, this means that \((t_{n})\) is bounded above. Thus, by the MCT, \((t_{n})\) converges and \(\limn t_n \geq 0\text{.}\) Therefore, by Proposition 9.2.4. \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty(a_j - b_j)\) converges to some nonpositive number. We conclude that \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty a_{j}\) converges, and the sum is \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty b_j + \sum_{j=1}^\infty (a_j - b_j)\text{,}\) whose value is at most \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty b_j\text{.}\) Finally, applying the same reasoning with \(-a_j\) in place of \(a_j\text{,}\) we conclude that

\begin{equation*} |\sum_{j=1}^\infty a_j| \leq \sum_{j=1}^\infty b_j\text{.} \end{equation*}

For example, since \(\left|\frac{\sin j}{2^{j}}\right|\leq \frac{1}{2^{j}}\) and \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty \frac{1}{2^{j}}\) converges by Example 9.2.2, we can deduce that \(\sum_{j=1}^\infty \frac{\sin j}{2^{j}}\) converges too.
