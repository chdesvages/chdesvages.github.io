Section 16.10 Exercises

The relevant exercises to this section are in Chapter 20 of Liebeck.

Checkpoint 16.10.1.

Consider the permutation

\begin{equation*} f=\left(\begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \amp 7 \amp 8 \cr3 \amp 5 \amp 4 \amp 8 \amp 7 \amp 6 \amp 2 \amp 1 \end{matrix} \right) \end{equation*}

Write \(f, f^{2}\text{,}\) \(f^{-1}\text{,}\) and \(f^{146}\) in cycle notation.

Solution.
\begin{equation*} f=(1 \; 3 \; 4 \; 8 )(2 \; 5 \;7 ) ,\;\; f^2 = (1 \; 4)(2 \; 7 \; 5)(3 \; 8 ),\;\; f^{-1} = (8 \; 4 \; 3 \; 1)(7 \; 5 \; 2) \end{equation*}

To compute \(f^{146}\text{,}\) note thatthe order of \(f\) is \(lcm(4,3)=12\text{,}\) and \(146 = 12\cdot 12 + 2\text{,}\) thus

\begin{equation*} f^{146} = (f^{12})^{12}\cdot f^2 = f^2 = (1 \; 4)(2 \; 7 \; 5)(3 \; 8 )\text{.} \end{equation*}
Checkpoint 16.10.2.

Given \(2\leq k\leq n\text{,}\) how many \(k\)-cycles are there in \(S_{n}\text{?}\)

Solution.

There are \({n \choose k}\) ways of picking numbers \(\{a_{1},...,a_{k}\}\) in \(\{1,2,...,n\}\text{.}\) Now we count how many cycles there are on these numbers via the multiplication principle. For a cycle \(f=(f_{1} \; \cdots f_{k})\) with \(f_{i}\in \{a_{1},...,a_{k}\}\text{,}\) remember that we also have

\begin{equation*} f=(f_{2} \; f_{3}\cdots f_{k} \; f_{1}) =(f_{3} \; f_{4} \cdots f_{k} \; f_{1} \; f_{2})\cdots \end{equation*}

and so we can assume that \(f_{1} = a_{1}\text{,}\) otherwise we can shift the numbers in the cycle as above until the first term in parentheses is \(a_{1}\text{.}\) Then there are \(k-1\) choices for what \(f_{2}\) can be, and after picking that there are \(k-2\) choices for what \(f_{3}\) can be, and so on. Thus, by the multiplication principle, there are \(k(k-1)(k-2)\cdots 1=(k-1)!\) many cycles using the numbers \(\{a_{1},...,a_{k}\}\text{,}\) and thus there are

\begin{equation*} {n \choose k} (k-1)! \end{equation*}

many \(k\) cylces in \(S_{n}\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 16.10.3.

How many order \(3\) permutations are there in \(S_{4}\text{?}\)

Solution.

The only way a permutation in \(S_{4}\) can have order \(3\) is if it is a \(3\)-cycle, so by the previous exercise, the number of such cycles is \({4 \choose 3}(3-1)!\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 16.10.4.

How many order \(3\) permutations are there in \(S_{6}\text{?}\)

Solution.

By looking at the cycle notation for a permutation \(f\in S_{6}\text{,}\) the only way it can have order \(3\) is if it is either a \(3\)-cycle or a product of two disjoint \(3\)-cycles. As in one of the previous exercises, there are \({6\choose 3}\cdot 2\) \(3\)-cycles. To count disjoint products of \(3\)-cycles, notice that for each \(3\)-cycle \(p\) there are \((3-1)!=2\) \(3\)-cycles \(q\) we can have on the remaining \(3\) numbers in \(\{1,...,6\}\) not appearing in \(p\text{.}\) If we count them in this way though, each product of two disjoint \(3\)-cycles is counted twice (once as \(pq\) and then as \(qp\)), so we have to divide our answer by \(2\text{,}\) so the number of products of disjoint \(3\) cycles is

\begin{equation*} \left( {6\choose 3} \cdot 2\right) \cdot 2\cdot\frac{1}{2} = {6\choose 3} \cdot 2\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus, the total number of order \(3\) permutations is \({6\choose 3} \cdot 2+ {6\choose 3} \cdot 2= {6\choose 3} \cdot 4\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 16.10.5.

We say a set \(S\subseteq S_n\) generates \(f\in S_{n}\) there are \(f_{1},...,f_{k}\in S\) so that \(f=f_{1}\cdots f_{k}\) (where we could have \(k=1\text{,}\) that is, this could just be a composition of just one function), and \(S\) generates \(S_{n}\) if it generates \(f\) for every \(f\in S_{n}\text{.}\)

  1. Show that if \(f\in S\text{,}\) then \(S\) generates \(\iota\) and \(f^{-1}\text{.}\) Solution.

    Recall that each permutation has an order \(r\text{,}\) and so \(\iota = f^{r}\) and \(f^{-1} = f^{r-1}\) are both products of elements of \(S\) (in particular, just products of \(f\) with itself over and over).

  2. Show that \(S=\{ (1\; 2), (2 \; 3),...,(n-1 \; n)\}\) generates \(S_{n}\text{.}\) Solution.

    Let \(f\in S_{n}\text{.}\) Since \(f\) is a composition of cycles by Proposition 20.3, we just need to show that each cycle is a composition of functions in \(S\text{.}\) So let \((a_{1}....a_{r})\) be one of the cycles. The claim will follow from the following subclaim:

    Subclaim: If \((a_{1}...a_{r})\) is an \(r\)-cycle, then it is a composition of functions from \(S\text{.}\)

    Note that by the proof of Lemma 16.9.1 (Proposition 20.6 in the book),

    \begin{equation*} (a_{1}\; \cdots \; a_{r}) = (a_{1} \; a_{r})\cdots (a_{1}\; a_{2})\text{,} \end{equation*}

    Thus, it suffices to show that the cycles \((1 \; j)\) where \(1\lt j\leq n\) are compositions of functions from \(S\text{.}\) We will prove this by induction on \(j=2,...,n\text{.}\)

    If \(j=2\text{,}\) then this is immediate since \((1\;2)\in S\) already. This proves the base case. Now we prove the induction step. Assume we have shown that \((1\; j)\) is a composition of functions from \(S\) for some \(1\lt j\lt n\text{.}\) Then

    \begin{equation*} (1 \; j+1) = (1 \; j)(j\; j+1)(1 \; j)\text{.} \end{equation*}

    We already have \((j\; j+1)\in S\text{,}\) and \((1\; j)\) is a composition of functions from \(S\) by the induction hypothesis, hence so is \((1 \; j+1)\text{,}\) which proves the induction step.

  3. Show that \(T=\{(1\; 2),(1\; 2 \; 3),..., (1 \; 2 \; ...\; n)\}\) generates \(S_{n}\text{.}\) Solution.

    Note that since \((1 \; 2 \; \cdots \; k-1 )\in T\text{,}\) \(T\) generates \((1 \; 2 \; \cdots \; k-1 )^{-1}\text{,}\) and so it also generates

    \begin{equation*} (1 \; 2 \; \cdots \; k-1 )^{-1} (1 \; 2 \; \cdots \; k-1 \; k) =(k-1 \; k) \end{equation*}

    for every \(k\text{.}\) We have already shown that every \(f\) is a product of elements in \(S_{n}\) is a product of permutations of the form \((k-1\; k)\text{,}\) and now we have shown that each such permutation is a product of elements in \(T\text{,}\) thus \(T\) generates \(S_n\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 16.10.6.

Given \(x,y\in S_{n}\text{,}\) we call \(z=xyx^{-1}\) the conjugate of \(y\) by \(x\). If \(z=xyx^{-1}\) for some \(x\in S_{n}\text{,}\) we say \(z\) is a conjugate of \(y\text{.}\)

(a) Show that the relation \(y\sim z\) if \(y\) is conjugate to \(z\) is an equivalence relation on \(S_{n}\text{.}\)

(b) Fix \(x\in S_{n}\text{.}\) Show that the function sending \(y\in S_{n}\) to \(xyx^{-1}\) is a bijection on \(S_{n}\text{.}\)

(c) If \(c=(a_{1} \; a_{2}...a_{k})\) is a cycle in \(S_{n}\) and \(x\in S_{n}\text{,}\) prove that

\begin{equation*} xcx^{-1}=(x(a_{1})...x(a_{k}))\text{.} \end{equation*}

(d) If \(y,z\) are conjugates, what can you say about their cycle shapes? Is the converse also true?

Solution.

(a) Clearly \(y\sim y\) since \(y=\iota y\iota^{-1}\text{,}\) so \(\sim\) is reflexive. If \(y\sim z\text{,}\) then \(y=xzx^{-1}\) for some \(x\in S_{n}\text{.}\) Composing both sides on the left with \(x^{-1}\text{,}\) we get \(x^{-1}y=zx^{-1}\text{,}\) and then composing again on the right gives \(x^{-1}yx=z\text{,}\) and so \(z\) is a conjugate of \(y\text{,}\) hence \(z\sim y\) and we have reflexivity. Finally, if \(y\sim z\) and \(z\sim w\text{,}\) then \(y=xzx^{-1}\) and \(z=sws^{-1}\) for some \(x,s\in S_{n}\text{.}\) Then \(y=xzx^{-1} = xswx^{-1}s^{-1} = xsw(xs)^{-1}\text{,}\) so \(y\sim w\text{.}\) This proves transitivity.

(b) Let \(F(y)=xyx^{-1}\text{.}\) Then \(F\) is surjective since, for every \(y\in S_{n}\text{,}\) \(y=F(x^{-1}yx)\text{.}\) It is injective since, if \(xyx^{-1}=xzx^{-1}\text{,}\) then we can compose both sides on the left with \(x^{-1}\) to get \(yx^{-1}=zx^{-1}\text{,}\) and then compose with \(x\) on the right to get \(y=z\text{.}\)

(c) If we plug in \(x(a_{i})\) (this is the function \(x\) evaluated at \(a_{i}\)), we get \(xcx^{-1}(x(a_{i}))=xc(a_{i})=x(a_{i+1})\) if \(i\lt k\) and when \(i=k\text{,}\) \(xcx^{-1}(x(a_{k}))=xc(a_{k})=x(a_{1})\text{.}\) Thus, \(xcx^{-1}=(x(a_{1})...x(a_{k}))\text{.}\)

In particular, \(xcx^{-1}\) is a cycle of the same length.

(d) If \(y,z\) are conjugates, then they have the same cycle-shape. If \(z=xyx^{-1}\text{,}\) and \(y\in S_{n}\) decomposes into disjoint cycles \(y=c_{1}\cdots c_{k}\text{,}\) then

\begin{equation*} xyx^{-1}=xc_{1}x^{-1} xc_{2}x^{-1}\cdots xc_{k}x^{-1}\text{,} \end{equation*}

so \(xyx^{-1}\) is also a composition of cycles of the same lengths, and so it has the same cycle-shape as \(y\text{.}\)

If \(y\) and \(z\) have the same cycle shape, then they are also conjugate. To see this, note that if \(y=c_1...c_{r}\) is a product of disjoint cycles, we can write \(z=d_{1}...d_{r}\) as a product of cycles so that \(d_{i}\) has the same length as \(c_{i}\text{.}\) If \(c_{i}=(a_{i,1}...a_{i,k_i})\) and \(d_{i}=(b_{i,j},...,b_{i,k_{i}})\text{,}\) define \(x\in S_{n}\) so that \(x(a_{i,j})=b_{i,j}\text{.}\) Then \(xc_ix^{-1}=(x(a_{i,1})...x(a_{i,k_i}))=(b_{i,1},...,b_{i,k_{i}})=d_i\text{,}\) and so \(xyx^{-1}=z\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 16.10.7.

Landau's function \(L(n)\) is defined for every \(n\in\mathbb{N}\) to be the largest order of an element of \(S_{n}\text{.}\) This has no convenient formula, but we will prove some simple bounds on it in the next two exercises.

(a) Show that for all \(n\in\mathbb{N}\text{,}\) \(L(n)\lt 2^{n}\text{.}\) Hint: it may help to show \(n\lt 2^{n}\) for all \(n\in\mathbb{N}\text{.}\)

Solution. 1

Claim: For all \(n\in \mathbb{N}\text{,}\) the order of any cycle \(f\in S_{n}\) is at most \(2^{n}\text{.}\)

Proof: We first need to show that for all natural numbers \(n\text{,}\)

\begin{equation} n\lt 2^{n}\text{.}\label{e_n_2_n}\tag{16.10.1} \end{equation}

(I have shown this in class, so students may also just cite the lectures for this fact.) This is true for the base case \(n=1\text{,}\) so suppose \(n\geq 1\) is an integer so that \(2^{n}>n\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} n+1\lt 2^{n}+1\lt 2^{n}+2^{n}=2^{n+1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

This proves the induction step and hence (1).

Let \(n\in\mathbb{N}\) and let \(f\in S_{n}\text{.}\) Then \(f\) can be written as a product of cycles of lengths \(r_{1},....,r_{k}\) where \(r_{i}\in \mathbb{N}\) and \(r_{1}+\cdots + r_{k}=n\text{.}\) Then the order is the least common multiple of these numbers, which is at most

\begin{equation*} r_{1}\cdots r_{k}\lt 2^{r_{1}}\cdots 2^{r_{k}}=2^{r_{1}+\cdots + r_{k}}=2^{n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Solution. 2

Claim: \(L(n)\geq\frac{n}{2}\cdot \left(\frac{n}{2}-1\right)\) for \(n\in \mathbb{N}\text{.}\)

Proof.

We split into two cases depending on if \(n\) is even or odd. If \(n\) is even, then \(n/2\) and \(n/2-1\) are coprime. Let \(f\) have cycle decomposition

\begin{equation*} f=(1\; 2\cdots n/2)(n/2+1 \;\; n/2+2...n-1)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then these two cycles have lengths \(n/2\) and \(n/2-1\) respectively, and the order of \(f\) is the least common multiple of these two numbers by Proposition 20.4, and since they are comprime, this must be \(\frac{n}{2}\left(\frac{n}{2}-1\right)\text{.}\)

If \(n\) is odd, then \(\frac{n-1}{2}\) and \(\frac{n+1}{2}\) are coprime. Let \(g\) have cycle decomposition

\begin{equation*} g=(1\; 2\cdots (n-1)/2)((n+1)/2 ... n) \end{equation*}

then the lengths of the cycles in this decomposition are \(\frac{n-1}{2}\) and \(\frac{n+1}{2}\text{,}\) and so the order of the cycle is the least common multiple of these numbers by Proposition 20.4 which (because they are coprime) is just their product: \(\frac{n-1}{2}\frac{n+1}{2}= \frac{n^2-1}{4}\text{.}\)

Taking the minimum of this and \(\frac{n}{2}\left(\frac{n}{2}-1\right)\text{,}\) we see that \(L(n)\geq \frac{n}{2}\left(\frac{n}{2}-1\right)\) for all \(n\in\mathbb{N}\text{.}\)
