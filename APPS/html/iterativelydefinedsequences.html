Section 8.2 Iteratively defined sequences

Iteratively or recursively defined sequences are those where the value of \(x_{n+1}\) is specified in terms of previous values of the sequence \(x_1, \dots, x_n\text{,}\) (most usually just in terms of \(x_n\)).

Example 8.2.1.

In high school you may have met recursively defined sequences of the form \(x_1 = c\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} x_{n+1} = a x_n +b \; \mbox{ for } \; n \geq 1 \end{equation*}

where \(a, b, c \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Show that when \(|a| \lt 1\text{,}\) \((x_n)\) converges and find its limit. Show that when \(|a|>1\text{,}\) \((x_n)\) diverges. What happens when \(a = \pm 1\text{?}\)

Solution: Let's first give ourselves a sneak preview of what \(L\) might be in terms of \(a,b\) and \(c\text{.}\) By the rules for limits, if the sequence \((x_n)\) converges to \(L\text{,}\) then \(L\) must satisfy \(L = aL + b\text{,}\) or equivalently \(L = \frac{b}{1-a}\text{.}\) Since we cannot divide by \(0\text{,}\) this already shows us that the case \(a=1\) is special.

Indeed, if \(a = 1\text{,}\) then \(L = aL + b\) becomes \(L = L + b\text{,}\) and unless \(b=0\) this cannot hold. So when \(a =1\text{,}\) the sequence diverges in the case that \(b \neq 0\text{,}\) and is the constant sequence \(x_n = c\) in the case that \(b = 0\text{.}\)

Now suppose \(a \neq 1\text{.}\) Since we are guessing the limit to be \(\frac{b}{1-a}\text{,}\) it makes sense to set \(y_n = x_n - \frac{b}{1-a}\) and to re-write the basic equation \(x_{n+1} = a x_n +b\) in the equivalent form \(y_{n+1} = a y_n\text{.}\) This in turn the same as \(y_n = (c - \frac{b}{1-a})a^{n-1}\text{,}\) as is easily established by induction. If \(|a|>1\text{,}\) \(|y_n|\) diverges to \(+\infty\) and so \((x_n)\) also diverges; if \(|a| \lt 1\text{,}\) then \(y_n \to 0\) as we have already seen in a previous example, and this is the same as \(x_n \to \frac{b}{1-a}\text{.}\) The remaining case \(a= -1\) we leave as an exercise.

The MCT is especially useful for proving convergence of some (but not all) iteratively or recursively defined sequences.

Example 8.2.2.

In 60AD, the Greek mathematician Hero of Alexandria wanted to approximate \(\sqrt{2}\) with rational numbers. He came up with the following recurrence relation, now called Heron's Method, for computing \(\sqrt{2}\text{.}\) Let \(x_{1}=2\) and for each \(n\in\mathbb{N}\text{,}\) let

\begin{equation*} x_{n+1}=\frac{1}{2}\left(x_{n}+\frac{2}{x_{n}}\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Let us imagine that we knew that \((x_n)\) converged to something, \(L\text{.}\) What must \(L\) satisfy? Well, by the rules for limits, since \(x_{n+1} \to L\) too, and assuming that \(L \neq 0\text{,}\) it must satisfy

\begin{equation*} L=\frac{1}{2}\left(L+\frac{2}{L}\right)\text{,} \end{equation*}

or \(2L =L + \frac{2}{L}\text{,}\) or \(L = \frac{L}{2}\text{,}\) which is the same as \(L^2 = 2\text{,}\) or \(L = \pm\sqrt{2}\text{.}\) We want to narrow this down so that the only possibility is \(L = + \sqrt{2}\text{,}\) and also so that we do indeed know that \((x_n)\) converges. Given that \(x_1 = 2 > \sqrt{2}\text{,}\) it makes sense to try to show that \((x_n)\) is decreasing, and is bounded below by \(\sqrt{2}\text{,}\) which is the same thing as \(x_n > 0\) and \(x_n^2 \geq 2\) for all \(n\text{.}\) Once we have this, the MCT will imply that \((x_n)\) does indeed converge to some number \(L\text{,}\) which must then satisfy \(L \geq 0\) and \(L^2 = 2\text{,}\) thus indeed \(L = + \sqrt{2}\text{.}\)

Let's do a bit of experimentation to see whether \((x_n)\) is decreasing or not by computing a few values (which you can do on a calculator or writing a simple program):

\begin{equation*} x_{1}=2,\;\; x_{2} = \frac{1}{2}\left(2+\frac{2}{2}\right) = \frac{3}{2}, \;\; x_{3}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{3}{2}+\frac{2}{3/2}\right) = \frac{17}{12}, \;\; x_{4} = \frac{237}{408}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(2 > 3/2 > 17/12 > 237/408\text{,}\) we see that

\begin{equation*} x_1 > x_2 > x_3 > x_4\text{,} \end{equation*}

and this suggests that the sequence might indeed be decreasing. Let's try to prove this. This is the same thing as showing \(x_{n}-x_{n+1}\geq 0\text{,}\) so let's look at this difference:

\begin{equation*} x_{n}-x_{n+1}=x_{n}-\frac{1}{2}\left(x_{n}+\frac{2}{x_{n}}\right) =\frac{x_{n}^{2}-2}{2x_{n}}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus, we just need to show that this last term is non-negative. The \(x_{n}\) are always positive, this is established by induction: \(x_1>0\text{,}\) and if \(x_n>0\text{,}\) then we see that \(x_{n+1}>0\) also straight from the definition. Hence, we just need to show that \(x_{n}^{2}\geq 2\) for all \(n\geq 1\text{.}\) This we also prove by induction! If \(n=1\) then \(x_{1}=2\text{,}\) and for \(n>1\text{,}\) it follows from the fact that for \(x \neq 0\)

\begin{equation*} \left(\frac{1}{2}\left(x+\frac{2}{x}\right)\right)^2 \geq 2\text{,} \end{equation*}

which, finally, is something that we can just check directly:

\begin{equation*} \left(\frac{1}{2}\left(x+\frac{2}{x}\right)\right)^2 \geq 2 \iff \left(x+\frac{2}{x}\right)^2 \geq 8 \iff x^2 + \frac{4}{x^2} + 4 \geq 8 \iff \frac{(x^2 - 2)^2}{x^2} \geq 0\text{,} \end{equation*}

and the last statement is clearly true. Hence, we conclude that the sequence \((x_n)\) is decreasing.

Notice that in proving that \((x_n)\) is decreasing we have already established that \(x_n > 0\) and \(x_n^2 \geq 2\) for all \(n\text{!}\) Thus we immediately conclude from the earlier remarks that \((x_n)\) converges to \(L= +\sqrt{2}\text{!}\)

Problem 8.2.3.

How might you modify Heron's formula to construct a sequence of rational numbers converging to \(\sqrt{7}\text{?}\) or to \(\sqrt{51}\text{?}\)

Note from the above argument that induction is a really helpful tool to have at our disposal when we are dealing with recursively defined sequences. We see this again in the next example.

The previous example had the feature that the relevant boundedness of the sequence was immediate: the terms were all positive (hence bounded below by zero). Let's do an example where the relevant boundedness is not so immediate:

Example 8.2.4.

Let \(x_{1}=1\) and \(x_{n+1}=1+\sqrt{x_{n}}\) for \(n \geq 1\text{.}\) Prove that \((x_{n})\) converges and find its limit.

As before we first give ourselves a sneak preview of what the limit \(L\) must satisfy if we assume it exists. By the rules for limits we must have

\begin{equation} L=\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}x_{n} =\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}x_{n+1}=\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}(1+\sqrt{x_{n}})=1+\sqrt{L}\text{.}\label{e_L_sqrtL_1}\tag{8.2.1} \end{equation}

So \(L\) must satisfy \((L-1)^2 = L\text{,}\) i.e. \(L^2 -3L +1=0\text{,}\) i.e. \((L-\frac{3}{2})^2 = \frac{5}{4}\text{,}\) and this equation has solutions \(L = \frac{3 \pm \sqrt{5}}{2}\text{.}\) Both of these are positive but \(\frac{3 -\sqrt{5}}{2} \lt 1 \lt \frac{3 + \sqrt{5}}{2}\text{.}\) Now \(x_1 = 1\) and it's easily proved by induction that \(x_n \geq 1\) for all \(n\text{.}\) So if \((x_n)\) converges, it must converge to \(\frac{3 + \sqrt{5}}{2}\text{.}\)

We hope that \((x_n)\)is increasing, so if we prove it is also bounded, the MCT will imply it converges, and by what we have said above, the limit must be \(\frac{3 + \sqrt{5}}{2}\text{.}\)

Let us prove it is increasing by using induction. We want that \(x_{n+1}\geq x_{n}\) for all \(n\in\mathbb{N}\text{.}\) For the base case, we have \(x_{2} = 1+\sqrt{1} > 1 = x_1\text{.}\) For the inductive step, assume for a certain \(n\in\mathbb{N}\) that \(x_{n+1}\geq x_{n}\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} x_{n+2}-x_{n+1} =1+\sqrt{x_{n+1}}-(1+\sqrt{x_{n}}) =\sqrt{x_{n+1}}-\sqrt{x_{n}} \end{equation*}

and since \(x_{n+1}\geq x_{n}\) by the inductive hypothesis, we then also have \(\sqrt{x_{n+1}}\geq \sqrt{x_{n}}\) by Proposition 3.5. So the above displayed expression is non-negative, and thus \(x_{n+2}\geq x_{n+1}\text{.}\) This proves the inductive step and hence that \((x_n)\) is increasing.

Now we need show it is bounded above. Since we expect it to converge to \(L = \frac{3 + \sqrt{5}}{2}\text{,}\) it makes sense to try to show that this \(L\) is an upper bound. Let us prove that \(x_{n}\leq L\) for all \(n\in\mathbb{N}\) by induction. We can verify the base case \(x_{1}=1\leq L\) is already true. For the inductive step, it turns out that the actual value of \(L\) won't be as useful as the identity \(L=1+\sqrt{L}\) we derived in equation (8.2.1). Assume we have proved \(x_{n}\leq L\) for some \(n\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} x_{n+1}=1+\sqrt{x_{n}}\leq 1+\sqrt{L}=L\text{.} \end{equation*}

This proves the inductive step, and hence proves that \(x_{n}\leq L\) for all \(n\text{.}\) Hence, \((x_n)\) is bounded above and is increasing, so the MCT implies it converges. As we showed above, it must converge to \(L = \frac{3 + \sqrt{5}}{2}\text{.}\)
