Section 16.9 Even and Odd permutations

The content of this section will be a little easier to talk about with a motivating example. Recall the 9-puzzle: this is a puzzle that involves a table with 9 numbers and a missing space in a 3 by 3 grid, like so.

\begin{equation*} \begin{matrix}\Box \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr1 \amp 4\amp 5 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix} \end{equation*}

where the box represents an empty space. The objective is to put all the numbers in order by sliding numbers into the empty space, creating a new empty space, and so on. So for example, the above puzzle can be solved using the following moves:

\begin{equation*} \begin{matrix}\Box \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr1 \amp 4\amp 5 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix} \longrightarrow \begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr \Box \amp 4\amp 5 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix} \longrightarrow \begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr4 \amp \Box \amp 5 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix} \longrightarrow \begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr4 \amp 5 \amp \Box \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix} \longrightarrow \begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp \Box \cr \end{matrix} \end{equation*}

This puzzle is a 3 by 3 variant of the 15-puzzle (on a 4 by 4 grid) that was famous in the late 1800s.

Not every arrangement of numbers can be solved. For example, there is no way of solving the puzzle below

\begin{equation*} \begin{matrix}\Box \amp 3 \amp 2 \cr1 \amp 4\amp 5 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

How do we know that a given arrangement of numbers and a space can be solved? The math in this section will give us a way of figuring this out.

We start with the following lemma.

Lemma 16.9.1.

Every permutation in \(\mathrm{S}_n\) is a product of cycles of length \(2\text{.}\)

Proof.

Let \(f\) be a permutation in \(\mathrm{S}_n\text{.}\) We can also assume that \(f\) is a cycle of length \(r\) since otherwise, any permutation in \(\mathrm{S}_n\) is a product of disjoint cycles, and we can just multiply together the respective products of 2-cycles we get.

So suppose \(f=(a_1\ a_2\ a_3\ \ldots a_{r-1}\ a_r)\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} f=\big(a_1\ a_2\ a_3\ \ldots a_{r-1}\ a_r\big)=\big(a_1\ a_{r}\big)\big(a_1\ a_{r-1}\big)\big(a_1\ a_{r-2}\big)\cdots \big(a_1\ a_{3}\big)\big(a_1\ a_{2}\big)\text{.} \end{equation*}

One can check this by plugging in various values \(a_i\text{.}\) If we plug in \(a_3\text{,}\) for example, remember that when plugging a number into a composition \(f_{1}...f_{n}\text{,}\) we plug the number into \(f_n\) first, then the resulting value into \(f_{n-1}\text{,}\) and so on. So in the above product for \(f\text{,}\) we first plug \(a_3\) into \((a_{1}\; a_{2})\text{,}\) which just returns \(a_{3}\text{.}\) Then we plug that into \((a_{1}\; a_{3})\text{,}\) which gives \(a_{1}\text{.}\) Then we plug that into \((a_{1}\; a_{4})\) ad we get \(a_{4}\text{.}\) But now \(a_{4}\) does not appear in any other \(2\)-cycle, and so they all return \(a_{4}\text{.}\) Hence, \(f(a_{3})=a_{4}\text{,}\) and we can show similarly that \(f(a_{i})=a_{i+1}\) for all \(i\) except \(r\) where \(f(a_{r})=a_{1}\text{.}\)

Note that the product of cycles in this Lemma is far from being unique, and the cycles of order \(2\) in this Lemma are not necessary disjoint.

We would like to define a permutation to be even if it is a product of even number of cycles of order \(2\text{,}\) and odd if it is a product of odd number of cycles of order \(2\text{.}\) However, it is not clear from this definition whether or not a permutation can be both even and odd at the same time. Hence, we will actually define the properties of being even and odd slightly differently, and then later deduce that even and odd permutations can be written as an even or odd number of \(2\)-cycles.

Our ad-hoc definition of even and odd permutations in \(S_{n}\) will rely on the following function of \(n\) variables \(x_{1},...,x_{n}\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} \Delta (x_{1},...,x_{n}) = \prod_{1\leq i\lt j\leq n} (x_{i}-x_{j})\text{,} \end{equation*}

that is, the product of all pairs \((x_{i}-x_{j})\) where \(1\leq i\lt j\leq n\text{.}\) So for example, in \(S_{3}\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \Delta (x_{1},...,x_{n}) = (x_{1}-x_{2})(x_{2}-x_{3})(x_{1}-x_{3})\text{.} \end{equation*}

Given a permutation \(f\in S_{n}\text{,}\) we will define \(f(\Delta)\) to be the function

\begin{equation*} f(\Delta)(x_{1},...,x_{n}) = \prod_{1\leq i\lt j\leq n} (x_{f(i)}-x_{f(j)})\text{.} \end{equation*}

Notice that \(f(\Delta)\) is actually just \(\pm \Delta\text{,}\) since all the permutation does is change some of the \((x_{i}-x_{j})\) to either \((x_{i}-x_{j})\) or \((x_{j}-x_{i})\text{.}\) We define the signature of \(f\) to be

\begin{equation*} \mbox{sgn} (f) = \frac{f(\Delta)}{\Delta}\text{,} \end{equation*}

that is, \(\mbox{sgn} (f) =1\) if \(f(\Delta)=\Delta\) and \(\mbox{sgn} (f) =-1\) if \(f(\Delta)=-\Delta\text{.}\)

Definition 16.9.2.

We say a permutation \(f\in S_{n}\) is even if \(\mbox{sgn} (f) =1\) and odd if \(\mbox{sgn} (f) =-1\text{.}\)

The above definition of even and odd and the signature may seem really technical to work with, but the lemma below will provide us with some short-cuts so that we never actually have to refer to \(n\)-variable polynomials:

Lemma 16.9.3.

Let \(n\in\mathbb{N}\text{.}\)

  1. The signature of \(\iota\) is \(1\) and any \(2\)-cycle is \(-1\text{.}\)

  2. For any \(f\) and \(g\) in \(\mathrm{S}_n\text{,}\) we have \(\mathrm{sgn}(fg)=\mathrm{sgn}(f)\mathrm{sgn}(g)\text{.}\)

  3. The signature of a cycle of length \(r\) is \((-1)^{r-1}\text{.}\)

  4. For every permutation \(f\in\mathrm{S}_n\text{,}\) let \(f=\sigma_{1}\sigma_{2}\cdots\sigma_{s}\) where \(\sigma_{1},\ldots,\sigma_{s}\) are disjoint cycles of lengths \(r_{1},\ldots,r_{s}\text{.}\) Then

    \begin{equation*} \mathrm{sgn}(f)=(-1)^{r_1-1}(-1)^{r_2-1}(-1)^{r_3-1}\cdots (-1)^{r_s-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

  5. If \(f\in S_{n}\text{,}\) then \(\mathrm{sgn}(f)=\mathrm{sgn}(f^{-1})\text{.}\)

Proof.

  1. First, \(\mbox{sgn} (\iota)=\frac{\Delta}{\Delta}=1\text{.}\) Next, let \((a\; b)\) be a \(2\)-cycle with \(a\lt b\text{.}\) Then the only terms \((x_{i}-x_{j})\) in the polynomial \(\Delta\) that change sign (i.e. are reversed) when we compute \((a\; b)(\Delta)\) are the terms

    \begin{equation*} \Big((x_{a}-x_{a+1})(x_{a}-x_{a+2})\cdots (x_{a}-x_{b})\Big) \Big((x_{a+1}-x_{b})(x_{a+2}-x_{b})\cdots (x_{b-1}-x_{b})\Big) \end{equation*}

    There are \(b-a\) terms in the first set of big parentheses and \(b-a-1\) in the second set, for a total of \(2(b-a)-1\text{,}\) an odd number, hence

    \begin{equation*} (a\; b)(\Delta) = (-1)^{2(b-a)-1} \Delta = -\Delta\text{,} \end{equation*}

    so \(\mbox{sgn} ((a\; b))=-1\text{.}\)

  2. Let \(f\) and \(g\) be two permutations. Notice that \(f(-\Delta)=-f(\Delta)\text{,}\) and so

    \begin{equation*} fg(\Delta) =f(g(\Delta)) = f(\mbox{sgn} (g)\Delta) = \mbox{sgn} (g) f(\Delta) =\mbox{sgn} (g)\mbox{sgn} (f)\Delta \end{equation*}

    and this implies \(\mathrm{sgn}(fg)=\mathrm{sgn}(f)\mathrm{sgn}(g)\)

  3. Now things get a lot easier: If \(f\) is an \(r\)-cycle, then by the proof of Lemma 16.9.1, it is a product of \(r-1\) many \(2\)-cycles \(\sigma_{1},...,\sigma_{r}\text{,}\) so by (a) and (b),

    \begin{equation*} \mbox{sgn} (f) = \mbox{sgn} (\sigma_{1})\cdots \mbox{sgn} (\sigma_{r}) =(-1)^{r-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

  4. The same proof as in (c) proves this one.

  5. Finally,

    \begin{equation*} 1= \mbox{sgn} (\iota) = \mbox{sgn} (ff^{-1})=\mbox{sgn} (f)\mbox{sgn} (f^{-1}) \end{equation*}

    and since the signature is always \(\pm 1\text{,}\) we can divide both sides by \(\mbox{sgn} (f^{-1})\) to get

    \begin{equation*} \mbox{sgn} (f)=\mbox{sgn} (f^{-1})^{-1} = \mbox{sgn} (f^{-1})\text{.} \end{equation*}

As a corollary of the above lemma, we get the following.

Corollary 16.9.4.

Let \(f\) be a permutation in \(\mathrm{S}_n\text{.}\)

  1. \(f\) is even if and only if it is a product of even number of cycles of order \(2\text{.}\)

  2. \(f\) is odd if and only if it is a product of odd number of cycles of order \(2\text{.}\)

Example 16.9.5.

The permutation

\begin{equation*} \left(\begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5 \cr2 \amp 3 \amp 1 \amp 5 \amp 4 \cr \end{matrix} \right)=\big(1\ 2\ 3\big)\big(4\ 5\big)=\big(1\ 3\big)\big(1\ 2\big)\big(4\ 5\big) \end{equation*}

is odd, because it is a product of \(3\) cycles of length \(2\text{.}\)

\(\left(\begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5 \cr2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5 \amp 1 \cr \end{matrix} \right)=\big(1\ 2\ 3\ 4\ 5\big)=\big(1\ 5\big)\big(1\ 4\big)\big(1\ 3\big)\big(1\ 2\big)\) is even.

Example 16.9.6.

Let's revisit the \(9\)-puzzle and start with the one we solved earlier, that is, where we wanted to rearrange

\begin{equation*} \begin{matrix}\Box \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr1 \amp 4\amp 5 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix} \;\;\;\; \mbox{ into } \;\;\;\; \begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp \Box \cr \end{matrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

If we consider the \(\Box\) as being \(9\text{,}\) the above arrangement on the left corresponds to applying the permutation

\begin{equation*} f=\left(\begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \amp 7 \amp 8 \amp 9 \cr4 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 5 \amp 6 \amp 9 \amp 7 \amp 8 \amp 1\cr \end{matrix} \right) =(1 \; 4 \; 5 \; 6 \; 9 )(2)(3)(7)(8)=(1 \; 4 \; 5 \; 6 \; 9 ) \end{equation*}

to the grid on the right. Performing a move by moving a number into the \(\Box\) corresponds to applying a \(2\)-cycle. Let's revisit the moves we did earlier to solve this puzzle and indicate what \(2\)-cycles we are using:

\begin{equation*} \begin{matrix}\Box \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr1 \amp 4\amp 5 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix} \;\; \stackrel{(1 \; 4)}{\longrightarrow} \;\; \begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr \Box \amp 4\amp 5 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix} \;\; \stackrel{(4 \; 5)}{\longrightarrow} \;\; \begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr4 \amp \Box \amp 5 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix} \;\; \stackrel{(5 \; 6)}{\longrightarrow} \;\; \begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr4 \amp 5 \amp \Box \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix} \;\; \stackrel{(6 \; 9)}{\longrightarrow} \;\; \begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp \Box \cr \end{matrix} \end{equation*}

Thus, applying the permutation \((6 \; 9)(5 \; 6)(4 \; 5)(1 \; 4)\) returns the board to the original order, that is, it inverts the permutation \((1 \; 4 \; 5 \; 6 \; 9 )\text{.}\)

Now let's look at the following puzzle:

\begin{equation*} \begin{matrix}\Box \amp 3 \amp 2 \cr1 \amp 4\amp 5 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix} \;\;\;\; \mbox{ into } \;\;\;\; \begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp \Box \cr \end{matrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The permutation corresponding to this arrangement is

\begin{equation*} g=\left(\begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \amp 7 \amp 8 \amp 9 \cr4 \amp 3 \amp 2 \amp 5 \amp 6 \amp 9 \amp 7 \amp 8 \amp 1\cr \end{matrix} \right) =(1 \; 4 \; 5 \; 6 \; 9 )(2\; 3)(7)(8)=(1 \; 4 \; 5 \; 6 \; 9 )(2 \; 3) \end{equation*}

Notice that if the puzzle can be solved, that requires moving the square up or down an even number of times, and left and right an even number of times. Thus, the puzzle must take an even number of moves, and since each move is a \(2\)-cycle, that implies that the inverse of the above permutation must be even, and hence the permutation itself must also be even (this uses Lemma 7.10 (a),(d) and (e)). However, the signature of the above permutation (using Lemma 7.10 (b) and (c)) is

\begin{equation*} \mbox{sgn} \Big((1 \; 4 \; 5 \; 6 \; 9)(2 \; 3)\Big) =\mbox{sgn} \Big((1 \; 4 \; 5 \; 6 \; 9)\Big)\mbox{sgn} \Big((2 \; 3)\Big) =(-1)^{5-1}(-1)^{2-1} = -1\text{.} \end{equation*}

and so this is an odd permutation, and this gives a contradiction. Thus, the original puzzle is not solvable.
