Section 15.5 The Binomial and Multinomial Theorems

A very natural place that binomial coefficients appear (and the reason why they are called binomial coefficients) is in the following theorem, with which you are probably already familiar from high school, and use of which we have already made in Weeks 3 and 4 of this course :

Theorem 15.5.1. The Binomial Theorem.

Let \(n\in\mathbb{N}\) and \(a,b\in \mathbb{C}\text{.}\) Then 1 

\begin{equation} (a+b)^{n} = \sum_{k=0}^{n} {n\choose k} a^{k}b^{n-k}\text{.}\label{e_binomialtheorem}\tag{15.5.1} \end{equation}
Liebeck assumes \(a,b\in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) but this is not necessary.

As an application, let's give a second (faster) proof of Corollary 15.3.4: by the Binomial Theorem,

\begin{equation*} 2^{n} = (1+1)^{n} = \sum_{k=0}^{n} {n\choose k} 1^{k}1^{n-k}=\sum_{k=0}^{n} {n\choose k}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Proof.

We have

\begin{equation*} (a+b)^n = \underbrace{(a + b)}_{1}\cdot \underbrace{(a+b)}_{2}\cdots \underbrace{(a+b)}_{n}\text{.} \end{equation*}

When we multiply out this product, we will get a sum of terms of the form \(a^k b^{n-k}\) for \(0 \leq k \leq n\text{.}\) How many of each of these do we get? Each bracket contributes either an \(a\) or a \(b\) in the final answer, and the number of \(a^k b^{n-k}\) terms in this final answer will be precisely the number of ways of picking \(k\) brackets from the brackets labelled \(1,2 ,\dots , n\) above. That is, it is \(n \choose k\text{.}\) In other words, the coefficient of \(a^k b^{n-k}\) in the expansion is exactly \(n \choose k\text{.}\)

The binomial theorem is a special case of the following theorem which is proved in much the same way:

Theorem 15.5.2. The Multinomial Theorem.

Let \(n\in\mathbb{N}\) and \(x_{1}, \dots ,x_{k}\in \mathbb{C}\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation} (x_{1}+\cdots + x_{k})^{n} = \sum {n\choose r_{1},\dots ,r_{k}} x_1^{r_{1}}\cdots x_{k}^{r_{k}}\label{e_multinomialtheorem}\tag{15.5.2} \end{equation}

where the sum is taken over all \(r_{i}\in \{0,1, \dots ,n\}\) such that \(r_{1}+\cdots + r_{k}=n\text{.}\)

Proof.

Let's number the parentheses in the product:

\begin{equation*} (x_{1}+\cdots + x_{k})^{n} = \underbrace{(x_{1}+\cdots + x_{k})}_{1}\cdot \underbrace{(x_{1}+\cdots + x_{k})}_{2}\cdots \underbrace{(x_{1}+\cdots + x_{k})}_{n}\text{.} \end{equation*}

When we multiply out this product, we will get terms of the form \(x_{1}^{r_{1}}\cdots x_{k}^{r_{k}}\) where \(r_{1}+\cdots + r_{k}=n\text{.}\) How many of these terms do we get? We can form such a product by picking a partition \(A_{1}, \dots ,A_{k}\) of \(\{1,2, \dots ,n\}\) with \(|A_i|=r_i\) and then picking \(x_j\) from \(A_i\) to include in our \(x_{1}^{r_{1}}\cdots x_{k}^{r_{k}}\) term if \(j\in A_i\text{.}\) Thus, the number of times \(x_{1}^{r_{1}}\cdots x_{k}^{r_{k}}\) appears when we multiply out the above product is the same as counting the number of ordered partitions \((A_{1}, \dots ,A_{k})\) of \(\{1,2, \dots ,n\}\) with \(|A_i|=r_i\text{,}\) which is \({n\choose r_{1}, \dots ,r_{k}}\text{.}\)

Example 15.5.3.

What is the coefficient of \(x\) in \((1+x+\frac{1}{x})^{5}\text{?}\)

Let \(a=1\text{,}\) \(b=x\) and \(c=\frac{1}{x}\text{.}\) When we expand \((a+b+c)^{5}\text{,}\) the terms of the form \(a^{r_{1}}b^{r_{2}}c^{r_{3}}\) (where \(r_{i}\geq 0\) and \(r_{1}+r_{2}+r_{3}=5\)) that equal \(x\) are of the form \(a^{4}b\text{,}\) \(a^{2}b^2c\text{,}\) and \(b^3c^{2}\text{,}\) so we just need to add together the coefficients of these terms, which by the multinomial theorem are

\begin{equation*} {5 \choose 4,1,0} + {5 \choose 2,2,1} + {5 \choose 0,3,2} =\frac{5!}{4!0!} + \frac{5!}{2!2!1!}+\frac{5!}{0!3!2!} =5+\frac{120}{4} + \frac{120}{12} = 5+30+10 = 45\text{.} \end{equation*}
