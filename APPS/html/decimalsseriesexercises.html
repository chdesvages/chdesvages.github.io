Section 9.4 Exercises

The relevant exercises in Liebeck are in Chapter 3.

Checkpoint 9.4.1.

Use the formula for a finite geometric series to show that for \(a, b \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(n \in \mathbb{N}\) we have

\begin{equation*} a^n - b^n = (a-b)(a^{n-1} + a^{n-2}b + \cdots + ab^{n-2} + b^{n-1})\text{.} \end{equation*}
Checkpoint 9.4.2.

Write each of the following decimal expansions in the form of a fraction \(\frac{p}{q}\) with \(p, q \in \mathbb{Z}\text{.}\)

  1. \(0.\overline{123}\) .

  2. \(0.\overline{2}\) .

  3. \(0.\overline{2331}\) .

Checkpoint 9.4.3.

Prove that if \(a_{n},b_{n}\in \{0,1,...,9\}\) are so that \(a_{n}+b_{n}\leq 9\) for all \(n\text{,}\) then

\begin{equation*} 0.a_{1}a_{2}\cdots + 0.b_{1}b_{2}\cdots = 0.(a_{1}+b_{1})(a_{2}+b_{2})..\text{.} \end{equation*}
Solution.

We use Proposition 9.2.4 and the definition of a decimal expansion to get

\begin{align*} 0.a_{1}a_{2}\cdots + 0.b_{1}b_{2}\cdots \amp =\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{a_{n}}{10^{n}} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{b_{n}}{10^{n}}\\ \amp = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left( \frac{a_{n}}{10^{n}} + \frac{b_{n}}{10^{n}} \right)\\ \amp = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{a_{n}+b_{n}}{10^{n}} =0.(a_{1}+b_{1})(a_{2}+b_{2})..\text{.} \end{align*}
Checkpoint 9.4.4.

Suppose that \(0 \leq a_1 \leq 8\) and that \(0.a_1 a_2 a_3\dots = 0.(a_1 +1) b_2 b_3 \dots\text{.}\) Show that \(a_j = 9\) and \(b_j = 0\) for all \(j \geq 2\text{.}\)

Solution.

By assumption,

\begin{equation*} \frac{a_1}{10} + \sum_{j=2}^\infty \frac{a_j}{10^j} = \frac{a_1 + 1}{10} + \sum_{j=1}^\infty \frac{b_j}{10^j}\text{,} \end{equation*}

so that

\begin{equation*} \sum_{j=2}^\infty \frac{a_j - b_j}{10^j} = \frac{1}{10}\text{.} \end{equation*}

But if \(a_j - b_j \lt 9\) for any \(j\) we will have

\begin{equation*} \sum_{j=2}^\infty \frac{a_j - b_j}{10^j} \lt \sum_{j=2}^\infty \frac{9}{10^j} = \frac{1}{10}\text{,} \end{equation*}

contradiction. So indeed \(a_j = 9\) and \(b_j = 0\) for all \(j \geq 2\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 9.4.5.

Suppose that \(a_{n}\geq 0\) and the numbers \(\sum_{j=1}^{n}a_{j}\) are bounded above. Is \(\sum_{j=1}^{\infty}a_{j}\) convergent?

Solution.

Yes, note that \(s_n=\sum_{j=1}^{n}a_{j}\) is an increasing sequence since

\begin{equation*} s_{n+1}-s_{n}=\sum_{j=1}^{n+1}a_{j}-\sum_{j=1}^{n}a_{j} = a_{n+1}\geq 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

By assumption, \((s_{n})\) is bounded above, so it must converge by the MCT.

Checkpoint 9.4.6.

In each case, either find a sequence \((a_n)\) such that \(a_n \geq 0\) for all \(n\) which has the desired property, or else explain why no such sequence exists.

(i) the sequence \((a_n)\) and the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) both converge

(ii) the sequence \((a_n)\) and the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) both fail to converge

(iii) the sequence \((a_n)\) converges and the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) fails to converge

(iv) the sequence \((a_n)\) fails to converge and the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) converges.

Solution.

(i) \(a_n = 2^{-n}\text{.}\)

(ii) \(a_n = 2^n\text{.}\)

(iii) \(a_n = 1/n\text{.}\) Then \(a_n \to 0\) but \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n}\) diverges — see his week's Lecture Quiz.

(iv) Not possible: by Proposition 9.2.3, if \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) converges, then the sequence \((a_n)\) converges (in fact it converges to \(0\)).

Checkpoint 9.4.7.

If \(\sum_n |a_n|\) converges, show that \(\sum_n a_n\) converges. Harder: Does the converse hold?

Solution.

This follows directly from the comparison test. The converse is not true: take \(a_n = \frac{(-1)^n}{n}\text{.}\) The convergence of \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) for this case is treated in Calculus and its Applications.

Checkpoint 9.4.8.

If \(a_n \to 0\text{,}\) does \(\sum_n a_n\) necessarily converge?

Solution.

No: take \(a_n = 1/n\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 9.4.9.

Suppose that \(|a_n - a_{n-1}| \leq 2^{-n}\) for all \(n \in \mathbb{N}\text{.}\) Show that the sequence \((a_n)\) converges. (Hint: Consider the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty (a_n - a_{n-1})\text{.}\))

Solution.

By the comparison test and the geometric series, we have that \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty (a_n - a_{n-1})\) converges. But the partial sums for this series are

\begin{equation*} s_n = \sum_{j=1}^n (a_j - a_{j-1}) = a_n - a_0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \((s_n)\) converges, so does \((a_n)\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 9.4.10.

A fun fact you'll learn how to prove if you take the 4th year course Fourier Analysis is that

\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2}=\frac{\pi^{2}}{6}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Given this fact, compute \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{(2n+1)^2}\text{.}\)

Solution.

Note that for any integer \(N\text{,}\)

\begin{align*} \sum_{n=1}^{2N+1}\frac{1}{n^{2}} \amp = \sum_{n=1}^{2N+1}\frac{1}{n^{2}} =\frac{1}{1^{2}}+\frac{1}{2^{2}}+\frac{1}{3^{2}}+\cdots + \frac{1}{(2N+1)^2}\\ \amp =\left(\frac{1}{1^{2}}+\frac{1}{3^{2}}+\cdots + \frac{1}{(2N+1)^2}\right) +\left(\frac{1}{2^{2}}+\frac{1}{4^{2}}+\cdots + \frac{1}{(2N)^2}\right)\\ \amp =\sum_{n=1}^{N}\frac{1}{(2n+1)^{2}}+ \sum_{n=1}^{N}\frac{1}{(2n)^{2}} = \sum_{n=1}^{N}\frac{1}{(2n+1)^{2}}+\frac{1}{2^2} \sum_{n=1}^{N}\frac{1}{n^{2}} \end{align*}

Now taking limits of both sides gives

\begin{align*} \frac{\pi^{2}}{6} = \amp \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2} =\lim_{N\rightarrow\infty}\sum_{n=1}^{2N+1}\frac{1}{n^{2}} =\lim_{N\rightarrow\infty} \left(\sum_{n=1}^{N}\frac{1}{(2n+1)^{2}}+\frac{1}{2^2} \sum_{n=1}^{N}\frac{1}{n^{2}}\right)\\ \amp =\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{(2n+1)^{2}}+ \frac{1}{2^2} \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^{2}} = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{(2n+1)^{2}}+ \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{\pi^{2}}{6} \end{align*}

and solving for the sum on the right gives

\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{(2n+1)^{2}}= \frac{\pi^{2}}{6} - \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{\pi^{2}}{6}=\frac{\pi^{2}}{8}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Checkpoint 9.4.11.

(Quite challenging.) Let \(a_n = \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n\text{,}\) and let \(b_n = 1 + \frac{1}{1!} + \frac{1}{2!} + \cdots + \frac{1}{n!}\text{.}\) Show that \((a_n)\) and \((b_n)\) both converge to the same limit. (You may assume that \((a_n)\) is convergent.)

Solution.

We have already seen in the previous exercise that

\begin{equation*} \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n \leq 1 + \frac{1}{1!} + \frac{1}{2!} + \cdots + \frac{1}{n!} \end{equation*}

so by the rules for limits we have \(\limn a_n \leq \limn b_n\text{.}\) (Note that \(\lim_n b_n\) exists by our discussion of the number \(e\text{.}\)) To show that \(\limn b_n \leq \limn a_n\) we must control \(1 + \frac{1}{1!} + \frac{1}{2!} + \cdots + \frac{1}{n!}\) by \(\left(1 + \frac{1}{m}\right)^m\) for some \(m\) (which will probably satisfy \(m \gg n\)). Indeed, note that for \(m \geq n\) we have

\begin{equation*} \left(1 + \frac{1}{m}\right)^m = 1 + 1 + \frac{m(m-1)}{2!m^2} + \frac{m(m-1)(m-2)}{3!m^3} + \dots + \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} + \dots + \frac{m(m-1) \dots (m-n+1)}{n! m^n} + \dots + \frac{m!}{m!m^m} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \geq 1 + 1 + \frac{1}{2!}\frac{m(m-1)}{m^2} + \frac{1}{3!}\frac{m(m-1)(m-2)}{m^3} + \dots + \frac{1}{n!}\frac{m(m-1) \dots (m-n+1)}{m^n}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The hope is that by taking \(m\) sufficiently large we should be able to take all of the terms

\begin{equation*} \frac{m(m-1)}{m^2}, \frac{m(m-1)(m-2)}{m^3}, \dots, \frac{m(m-1) \dots (m-n+1)}{m^n} \end{equation*}

very close to \(1\text{.}\)

Fix \(n\) and fix \(\epsilon>0\text{.}\) Since \(\frac{m(m-1)}{m^2} \to 1\) as \(m \to \infty\) there is an \(M_1\) such that for \(m > M_1\) we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{m(m-1)}{m^2} > 1- \epsilon\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(\frac{m(m-1)(m-2)}{m^3}\to 1\) as \(m \to \infty\) there is an \(M_2\) such that for \(m > M_2\) we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{m(m-1)(m-2)}{m^3} > 1- \epsilon\text{.} \end{equation*}

Continuing in the same way, we eventually obtain an \(M_{n-1}\) such that for \(m > M_{n-1}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{m(m-1) \dots (m-n+1)}{m^n} > 1-\epsilon\text{.} \end{equation*}

So for \(m > M :=\max\{M_1, \dots, M_{n-1}\}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{m(m-1) \dots (m-j+1)}{m^j} >1-\epsilon \end{equation*}

for all \(2\leq j \leq n\text{.}\) Hence, for \(m >M\) we have

\begin{equation*} \left(1 + \frac{1}{m}\right)^m \geq 1 + 1 +(1 - \epsilon)\left( \frac{1}{2!} + \cdots + \frac{1}{n!}\right) \geq (1 - \epsilon)\left(1 + \frac{1}{1!} + \frac{1}{2!} + \cdots + \frac{1}{n!}\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Therefore

\begin{equation*} \lim_{m \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{1}{m}\right)^m \geq (1 - \epsilon) \limn \left(1 + \frac{1}{1!} + \frac{1}{2!} + \cdots + \frac{1}{n!} \right)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Checkpoint 9.4.12.

Show that for every real number \(x\) there is a unique integer \(a_0 \in \mathbb{Z}\) such that \(a_0 \leq x \lt a_0 +1\text{.}\) (We sometimes call \(a_0\) the integer part of \(x\text{,}\) or the “floor” or “staircase” of \(x\text{,}\) denoted by \(a_0 = \lfloor x \rfloor\text{.}\)) Deduce that every real number \(x\) has a decimal expansion \(x = a_0.a_1 a_2 \dots a_n \dots\) where \(a_0 \in \mathbb{Z}\) and \(a_n \in \{0,1,2, \dots, 9\}\text{.}\)

Solution.

Let \(x \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) If \(x \in \mathbb{Z}\) we simply take \(a_0 = x\) and we are finished. So assume \(x \notin \mathbb{Z}\text{.}\) Let \(A= \{ n \in \mathbb{Z} \; | \; n > x\}\text{.}\) Then \(A \neq \emptyset\) by the Archimedean property, and \(x\) is a lower bound for \(A\text{.}\) Then \(A\) has a minimum member \(n_0\text{.}\) Therefore \(x \lt n_0\) and \(n_0 - 1 \leq x\text{.}\) Take \(a_0 = n_0 -1\text{.}\) (Note that this argument works if and only if we are in the setting of an ordered field with the Archimedean property.) The uniqueness of \(a_0\) is easy.

(The fact that \(A\) actually has a minimum member deserves more discussion. \(A\) is nonempty and has some lower bound. By the Archimedean property we can assert that it has a lower bound in \(\mathbb{Z}\text{.}\) The well-ordering principle — which states that a nonempty subset of \(\mathbb{N}\) has a least member, and which follows from the principle of induction — allows us to conclude that the minimum exists.)

For the second assertion, let \(a_{0}\) be the integer part of \(x\text{.}\) Then \(a_{0}\leq x\lt a_{0}+1\text{.}\) So \(x\in [a_{0},a_{0}+1)\text{,}\) i.e. \(x - a_0 \in [0,1)\text{.}\) Now apply Theorem 9.1.2 to \(x - a_0\text{.}\)

Formulate and prove results analogous to Theorems 9.1.3 and Theorem 9.1.8 for arbitrary \(x \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
