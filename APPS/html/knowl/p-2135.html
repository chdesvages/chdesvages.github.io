Now suppose we have show that for some \(K\lt n\) and \(k=0,1,...,K\text{,}\) any cycle for which \(f(j)=j\) for \(n-k\) distinct integers \(j\in \{1,2,...,n\}\) can be written as a product of disjoint cycles. Note that the sequence \(1,f(1),f(2),..\text{.}\) must eventually repeat by the pigeonhole principle, that is, since \(f(j)\) only takes on values \(\{1,2,...n\}\text{,}\) we must have that \(f^i(1)=f^j(1)\) for some \(0\leq i\lt j\leq n\text{.}\) Thus,

\begin{equation*} 1= f^{-i}f^{i}(1) = f^{-i}f^{j}(1) = f^{j-i}(1)\text{.} \end{equation*}

and so if we let \(\ell=j-i-1\) and \(a_{r}=f^{r}(1)\) for \(r=0,...,\ell\text{,}\) we then get that

\begin{equation*} f=(a_{1}\; a_{2}\;\cdots \; a_{\ell}) g \end{equation*}

where \(g(k)=k\) for \(k\in \{a_{1},...,a_{\ell}\}\) and \(g(k)=f(k)\) for every other \(k\in \{1,2,...,n\}\text{.}\) By the strong induction hypothesis, we can decompose \(g\) as a product of disjoint cycles, and since \(g(k)=k\) for \(k\in \{a_{1},...,a_{\ell}\}\text{,}\) they will also be disjoint from the cycle \((a_{1}\; a_{2}\;\cdots \; a_{\ell})\text{.}\) Composing all these cycles gives us \(f\text{.}\) This proves the induction step.
