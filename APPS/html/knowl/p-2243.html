If \(n\) is odd, then \(\frac{n-1}{2}\) and \(\frac{n+1}{2}\) are coprime. Let \(g\) have cycle decomposition

\begin{equation*} g=(1\; 2\cdots (n-1)/2)((n+1)/2 ... n) \end{equation*}

then the lengths of the cycles in this decomposition are \(\frac{n-1}{2}\) and \(\frac{n+1}{2}\text{,}\) and so the order of the cycle is the least common multiple of these numbers by Proposition 20.4 which (because they are coprime) is just their product: \(\frac{n-1}{2}\frac{n+1}{2}= \frac{n^2-1}{4}\text{.}\)
