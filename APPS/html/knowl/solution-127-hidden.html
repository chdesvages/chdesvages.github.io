We note that this is easily completely factorized over the complex numbers. Let \(\xi = e^{\frac{2\pi i}{10}}\text{,}\) then the \(5\)th roots of unity are \(1,\xi,\xi^2, \xi^3,\xi^4\text{.}\) By examining these numbers, we can see that \(\xi^{4}=\overline{\xi}\) and \(\xi^{3} = \overline{\xi}^{2}\text{.}\) Thus,

\begin{equation*} x^5+1 = (x+1)\underbrace{(x-\xi)(x-\bar{\xi})}_{\textrm{conjugate} }\underbrace{(x-\xi^2)(x-\bar{\xi}^2)}_{\textrm{conjugate} }\text{.} \end{equation*}

If we collect into pairs the terms which involve a root and its conjugate, we obtain real polynomials.

We compute:

\begin{equation*} (x-\xi)(x-\bar{\xi}) = x^2 - (\xi+\bar{\xi})x + \xi\bar{\xi} = x^2 - 2\cos(\frac{2\pi}{5})x+1 = x^2 +\frac{1-\sqrt{5}}{2}x+1\text{.} \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} (x-\xi^2)(x-\bar{\xi}^2) = x^2 -(\xi^2 + \bar{\xi}^2)x + \xi^2\bar{\xi}^2 = x^2 - 2\cos(\frac{4\pi}{5})x+1 = x^2 +\frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}x+1.\text{,} \end{equation*}

Hence,

\begin{equation*} x^5 = \left(x^2 +\frac{1-\sqrt{5}}{2}x+1\right) \left(x^2 +\frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}x+1\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}
