There are \({n \choose k}\) ways of picking numbers \(\{a_{1},...,a_{k}\}\) in \(\{1,2,...,n\}\text{.}\) Now we count how many cycles there are on these numbers via the multiplication principle. For a cycle \(f=(f_{1} \; \cdots f_{k})\) with \(f_{i}\in \{a_{1},...,a_{k}\}\text{,}\) remember that we also have

\begin{equation*} f=(f_{2} \; f_{3}\cdots f_{k} \; f_{1}) =(f_{3} \; f_{4} \cdots f_{k} \; f_{1} \; f_{2})\cdots \end{equation*}

and so we can assume that \(f_{1} = a_{1}\text{,}\) otherwise we can shift the numbers in the cycle as above until the first term in parentheses is \(a_{1}\text{.}\) Then there are \(k-1\) choices for what \(f_{2}\) can be, and after picking that there are \(k-2\) choices for what \(f_{3}\) can be, and so on. Thus, by the multiplication principle, there are \(k(k-1)(k-2)\cdots 1=(k-1)!\) many cycles using the numbers \(\{a_{1},...,a_{k}\}\text{,}\) and thus there are

\begin{equation*} {n \choose k} (k-1)! \end{equation*}

many \(k\) cylces in \(S_{n}\text{.}\)
