Section 11.5 Exercises

Checkpoint 11.5.1.

Find the (complex) roots of the following polynomials:

(a) \(x^2-5x+7-i=0\text{.}\)

Solution. 1

Recall that the roots of this polynomial are

\begin{equation*} x= \frac{5\pm z}{2} \end{equation*}

where \(z\) are the solutions to

\begin{equation*} z^2 = 5^2-4\cdot (7-i)\cdot 1 = 25-28+4i = -3+4i\text{.} \end{equation*}

If we set \(z=a+ib\text{,}\) this gives

\begin{equation*} a^2-b^2+2abi = -3+4i\text{.} \end{equation*}

So in particular, \(2ai = 4i\) and \(a^2-b^2 = -3\text{.}\) Moreover,

\begin{equation*} |z^2|= |-3 + 4i| = 5 \end{equation*}

Thus,

\begin{equation*} 5=|z^2|=|z|^2 = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}^2 = a^2+b^2 \end{equation*}

Adding this to \(a^2-b^2=-3\) implies \(2a^2 = 2\text{,}\) so \(a^2=1\text{,}\) and so \(a = \pm 1\text{.}\) Similarly, subtracting \(a^2-b^2=-3\) from the above equation gives \(2b^2 = 8\text{,}\) so \(b=\pm 2\text{.}\) Finally, \(2ab = 4\) implies that \(a\) and \(b\) have the same algebraic sign, so we must have \(a+ib\) is \(1+2i\) or \(-1-2i\) as our two solutions for \(z\text{.}\) Hence,

\begin{equation*} x=\frac{5 \pm (1+2i)}{2} \end{equation*}

so \(x\) is either \(3+i\) or \(2-i\text{.}\)

We can substitute these in to check, e.g. (just the first)

\begin{equation*} (3+i)^2-5(3+i)+7-i = (8+6i)-5(3+i)+7-i =0 \end{equation*}
Solution. 2

Let \(y=x^2\text{.}\) Then \(y^2+y+1=0\text{,}\) and the solutions to this are

\begin{equation*} x^2=y=\frac{1\pm \sqrt{5}}{2} \end{equation*}

Thus, we see that \(x=\pm \sqrt{\frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}}\) are two solutions, we just need to find the other two. They will be solutions to \(x^2 = \frac{1-\sqrt{5}}{2} = - \frac{\sqrt{5}-1}{2}\text{,}\) which are \(\pm i \sqrt{\frac{\sqrt{5}-1}{2}}\text{.}\) Thus, all 4 solutions are

\begin{equation*} \pm \sqrt{\frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}}, \;\; \pm i \sqrt{\frac{\sqrt{5}-1}{2}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Solution. 3

Since this is a real polynomial, the conjugate of \(1+i\) is also a root, so two of the root are \(1\pm i\text{.}\) Hence, the polynomial contains

\begin{equation*} (x-(1+i))(x-(1-i))= x^2-2x+2 \end{equation*}

as a factor. Now let's do polynomial long division to find the other factor. The leading factor of our original polynomial is \(2x^{4}\text{,}\) which we can eliminate by subtracting \(2x^2(x^2-2x+2)\) to get

\begin{equation*} -x^2+2x-2=-(x^2-2x+2) \end{equation*}

Then finally we can just subtract \(-1\cdot (x^2-2x+2)\) to eliminate this. Thus, we can factor the above polynomial as

\begin{equation*} 2x^4-4x^3+3x^2+2x-2=(2x^2-1)(x^2-2x+2)=2(x-2^{-1/2})(x+2^{1/2})(x-(1+i))(x+(1-i)) \end{equation*}

so the roots are finally \(\pm 2^{-1/2}, 1\pm i\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 11.5.2.

Factor the following polynomials into products of real polynomials that are linear and/or quadratic:

  • \(x^3-1\text{.}\) Solution.

    We first need to find the roots of unity of \(x^3\text{,}\) which are \(1, w=e^{2\pi i/3}=-\frac{1}{2}+\sqrt{3}{2}i\) and \(w^2=e^{4\pi i/3} = -\frac{1}{2}-\sqrt{3}{2}i=\overline{w}\text{.}\) Thus,

    \begin{align*} x^3-1 \amp = (x-1)(x-w)(x-\overline{w}) = (x-1)(x^2-wx-\overline{w}x+\overline{w}w)\\ \amp = (x-1)(x^2+x+|w|^2) = (x-1)(x^2+x+1)\text{.} \end{align*}

  • \(x^3+1\text{.}\) Solution.

    We need to find the roots of \(x^3+1=0\text{,}\) which are solutions to \(x^3=-1=e^{\pi i}\text{.}\) One solution is clearly \(1\text{.}\) Another we can get by taking 1/3 the exponent of \(e^{\pi i}\text{,}\) which is \(w=e^{\pi i/3} = \frac{1}{2}+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\text{.}\) We know that roots of real polynomials come in conjugate pairs, and so the other root is \(\overline{w}\text{.}\) Thus,

    \begin{align*} x^3+1 \amp = (x-(-1))(x-w)(x-\overline{w}) = (x+1)(x^2-wx-\overline{w}x+\overline{w}w)\\ \amp = (x+1)(x^2-x+|w|^2) = (x+1)(x^2-x+1)\text{.} \end{align*}

  • \(x^4-1\text{.}\) Solution.

    There is no need to find complex roots here:

    \begin{equation*} x^4-1 = (x^2)^2-1 = (x^2-1)(x^2+1)\text{.} \end{equation*}

  • \(x^4+1\text{.}\) Solution.

    To find the roots of \(x^4+1\text{,}\) we need to find solutions to \(x^4=-1=e^{\pi i}\text{.}\) One solution is \(z=e^{\pi i/4}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}+\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}i\text{.}\) To find all 4 roots, we just multiply this by the 4th roots of unity, which are \(\pm 1, \pm 1\text{,}\) thus all solutions which will be

    \begin{equation*} \pm \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \pm \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}i \end{equation*}

    where we consider all 4 possible combinations of \(+\)'s and \(-\)'s. Let \(z=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}+i\frac{1}{sqrt{2}}\) and \(w=-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}+i\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\text{.}\) Then the roots are \(z,w,\overline{z},\overline{w}\text{.}\) Thus,

    \begin{align*} x^4+1 \amp = (x-z)(z-\overline{z})(x-w)(x-\overline{w})\\ \amp =(x^2 -zx-\overline{z}x+z\overline{z})(x^2 -wx-\overline{w}x+w\overline{w})\\ \amp = (x^2-\sqrt{2}x+|z|^2)(x^2+\sqrt{2}x+|w|^2)\\ \amp = (x^2-\sqrt{2}x+1)(x^2+\sqrt{2}x+1)\text{.} \end{align*}

  • \(x^5+1\text{.}\) Hint: \(\cos \frac{2\pi}{5}= \frac{-1+\sqrt{5}}{4}\) and \(\cos \frac{4\pi}{5}= \frac{-1-\sqrt{5}}{4}\text{.}\) Solution.

    We note that this is easily completely factorized over the complex numbers. Let \(\xi = e^{\frac{2\pi i}{10}}\text{,}\) then the \(5\)th roots of unity are \(1,\xi,\xi^2, \xi^3,\xi^4\text{.}\) By examining these numbers, we can see that \(\xi^{4}=\overline{\xi}\) and \(\xi^{3} = \overline{\xi}^{2}\text{.}\) Thus,

    \begin{equation*} x^5+1 = (x+1)\underbrace{(x-\xi)(x-\bar{\xi})}_{\textrm{conjugate} }\underbrace{(x-\xi^2)(x-\bar{\xi}^2)}_{\textrm{conjugate} }\text{.} \end{equation*}

    If we collect into pairs the terms which involve a root and its conjugate, we obtain real polynomials.

    We compute:

    \begin{equation*} (x-\xi)(x-\bar{\xi}) = x^2 - (\xi+\bar{\xi})x + \xi\bar{\xi} = x^2 - 2\cos(\frac{2\pi}{5})x+1 = x^2 +\frac{1-\sqrt{5}}{2}x+1\text{.} \end{equation*}

    and

    \begin{equation*} (x-\xi^2)(x-\bar{\xi}^2) = x^2 -(\xi^2 + \bar{\xi}^2)x + \xi^2\bar{\xi}^2 = x^2 - 2\cos(\frac{4\pi}{5})x+1 = x^2 +\frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}x+1.\text{,} \end{equation*}

    Hence,

    \begin{equation*} x^5 = \left(x^2 +\frac{1-\sqrt{5}}{2}x+1\right) \left(x^2 +\frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}x+1\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Checkpoint 11.5.3.

If \(x^3+15x^2+74x+120\) has roots of the form \(a,a+1,a+2\text{,}\) find \(a\text{.}\)

Solution.

We see that by the Root-Coefficient Theorem,

\begin{equation*} -15=a+a+1+a+2=3a+3 \end{equation*}

and so \(a=-6\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 11.5.4.

Let \(a\in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) If \(x^{3}-x+a\) has three integer roots, solve for \(a\text{.}\)

Solution.

Note that if the integer roots are \(r_{1},r_{2}\text{,}\) and \(r_{3}\text{,}\) then by Proposition 7.1,

\begin{equation*} r_{1}+r_{2}+r_{3}=0 \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} r_{1}r_{2}+r_{2}r_{3}+r_{3}r_{1}=-1 \end{equation*}

Hence,

\begin{equation*} r_{1}^{2}+r_{2}^{2}+r_{3}^{2} = (r_{1}+r_{2}+r_{3})^{2}-2(r_{1}r_{2}+r_{2}r_{3}+r_{3}r_{1})=2\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since the \(r_{i}\) are integers, the \(r_{i}^{2}\) are nonnegative integers, and so one of them has to be zero. Thus, \(0=0^{2}-0+a\text{,}\) hence \(a=0\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 11.5.5.

Show that if \(z_1,z_2,z_3\in \C\) are so that \(z_1+z_2+z_3=0\text{,}\) and \(z_1^2+z_2^2+z_3^2=0\) then \(|z_1|=|z_2|=|z_3|\)

Solution.

Let \(f(z)=(z-z_1)(z-z_2)(z-z_3)\text{.}\) Then \(f(z)=z^3-az^2+bz-c\) where

\begin{align*} a\amp =z_1+z_2+z_3\\ b\amp =z_1z_2+z_2z_3+z_3z_1\\ c\amp =z_1z_2z_3 \end{align*}

By assumption, we get that \(a=z_1+z_2+z_3=0\text{.}\) Also, since \(z_{1}^2+z_{2}^2+z_{3}^2=0\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} (z_1+z_2+z_3)^2=z_1^2+z_2^2+z_3^2+2(z_1z_2+z_2z_3+z_3z_1) \end{equation*}

and so \(b=0\text{.}\) Thus, \(f(z)=z^3-c\text{,}\) so in particular, since \(z_{1},z_{2}\) and \(z_{3}\) are roots, we have \(z_{1}^3=z_{2}^3=z_{3}^{3}=c\text{.}\) Thus

\begin{equation*} |c|=|z_{i}^3|=|z_{i}|^3 \end{equation*}

so \(|z_{i}|=|c|^{1/3}\) for \(i=1,2,3\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 11.5.6.

Suppose \(|z_{1}|=|z_{2}|=|z_{3}|=1\) and \(z_{1}+z_{2}+z_{3}=0\text{.}\) Show that \(z_{1}^3=z_{2}^3=z_{3}^3\text{.}\) Hint: First think about when \(z_{1}=1\text{,}\) then use that to prove the general case.

Solution.

First let's assume \(z_{1}=1\text{.}\) Then \(z_{2}+z_{3}=-1\text{.}\) In particular, this means that the imaginary parts of \(z_{2}\) and \(z_{3}\) cancel, that is, if \(z_{j}=x_{j}+iy_{j}\text{,}\) then \(y_{2}=-y_{3}\text{.}\) Also, \(x_{2}+x_{3}=-1\text{,}\) and we can't have \(x_{2}>0\text{,}\) since then \(x_{2}+x_{3}>x_{3}\geq -1\text{.}\) Similarly, we can't have \(x_{3}>0\text{,}\) thus \(x_{2},x_{3}\leq 0\text{.}\) Also,

\begin{equation*} -x_{2} = \sqrt{1-y_{2}^2} = \sqrt{1-y_{3}^2}= -x_{3}\text{,} \end{equation*}

hence \(z_{3} = -x_{2} -iy_{2} = \overline{z_{2}}\text{,}\) and \(-1=z_{2}+z_{3}=-x_{2}-x_{2}=-2x_{2}\text{,}\) thus \(x_{2}=-\frac{1}{2}\text{.}\) Thus, we must have that \(z_{3} = -\frac{1}{2}\pm i\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\text{.}\)

For the general case, let \(w_{i}=\overline{z_{1}}z_{i}\text{,}\) then \(w_{1}=1\) and \(w_{1}+w_{2}+w_{3}=0\text{.}\) We now apply the previous case to conclude that \(w_{i} = w^{i}\) where \(w=e^{2\pi i/3}\text{.}\) Thus,

\begin{equation*} z_{j}^3 = (z_{1} w_{j})^3 = z_{1}^3 w_{j}^3 = z_{1} (w^{3j})=z_{1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Checkpoint 11.5.7.

Suppose \(|z_{1}|=|z_{2}|=|z_{3}|=1\) and \(z_{1}+z_{2}+z_{3}=0\text{.}\) Show that \(z_{1}^{2^{n}}+z_{2}^{2^{n}}+z_{3}^{2^{n}}=0\) for all \(n\in \mathbb{N}\text{.}\)

Solution.

By the previous problem, \(z_1,z_2,z_3\) are roots of \(z^3-c\) for some complex number \(c\) with \(|c|=1\text{.}\) In particular, by the Root-Coefficient Theorem,

\begin{equation*} z_{1}z_{2}+z_{1}z_{2}+z_{2}z_{3}=0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus,

\begin{equation*} 0=(z_{1}+z_{2}+z_{3})^2 = z_{1}^2+z_{2}^2+z_{3}^2 + 2(z_{1}z_{2}+z_{1}z_{2}+z_{2}z_{3})=z_{1}^2+z_{2}^2+z_{3}^2\text{.} \end{equation*}

Now we can repeat the process by induction using \(z_{1}^2,z_{2}^2,z_{3}^2\) instead of \(z_1,z_2,z_3\text{.}\)
