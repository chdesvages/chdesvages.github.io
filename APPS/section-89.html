Section 16.5 Powers

Let \(f\) be any permutation in \(\mathrm{S}_n\text{,}\) let \(m\) be any positive integer. Let

\begin{equation*} f^{m}=\underbrace{f\circ f\circ f\circ\cdots\circ f\circ f}_{m\ \mathrm{times}}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We also let \(f^{-m}=(f^{-1})^m\text{,}\) and \(f^{0}=\iota\text{.}\) Then we have the familiar power rules for compositions as we do for multiplication: for \(r,s\in\mathbb{Z}\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} f^{r}f^{s}=f^{r+s}\ \text{ and } \ \Big(f^{r}\Big)^{s}=f^{rs}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Example 16.5.1.

Let \(f=\left(\begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5\cr2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5 \amp 1\cr \end{matrix} \right)\in\mathrm{S}_5\text{.}\) Then, one can check

\begin{equation*} f^{-1}=\left(\begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5\cr5 \amp 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4\cr \end{matrix} \right),\ f^{0}=\iota,\ f^{1}= f,\ f^{2}=\left(\begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5\cr3 \amp 4 \amp 5 \amp 1 \amp 2\cr \end{matrix} \right)\text{,} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} f^{3}=\left(\begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5\cr4 \amp 5 \amp 1 \amp 2 \amp 3\cr \end{matrix} \right),\ f^{4}= f^{-1},\ f^{5} = \iota, f^{6}= f, f^{7}= f^2, \ldots\text{.} \end{equation*}
Checkpoint 16.5.2.

Let \(f=\left(\begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5\cr2 \amp 1 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5\cr \end{matrix} \right)\in\mathrm{S}_5\text{.}\) What is \(f^{2019}\text{?}\)

Solution.

Since \(f^{2}=\iota\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \underbrace{f\circ f\circ f\circ\cdots\circ f\circ f}_{2019\ \mathrm{times}}=\underbrace{f\circ f\circ f\circ\cdots\circ f\circ f}_{1009\times 2\ \mathrm{times}}\circ f \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} =\Big(f\circ f\Big)^{1009}\circ f=\iota^{1009}\circ f= f\text{.} \end{equation*}

Let us summarize some of the properties about permutations in \(\mathrm{S}_{n}\) we have proved.

Lemma 16.5.3.

The set \(\mathrm{S}_n\) equipped with the composition rule \(\circ\) has the following properties:

  • Closure: for any \(f\) and \(g\) in \(\mathrm{S}_n\text{,}\) one has \(f\circ g\in\mathrm{S}_n\text{,}\)

  • Associativity: for any \(f\text{,}\) \(g\text{,}\) and \(h\) in \(\mathrm{S}_n\text{,}\) one has

    \begin{equation*} f\circ \Big(g\circ h\Big)=\Big(f\circ g\Big)\circ h\text{,} \end{equation*}

  • Identity element: there is unique permutation \(\iota\in\mathrm{S}_n\) such that

    \begin{equation*} f\circ\iota=\iota\circ f=f \end{equation*}

    for any \(f\in\mathrm{S}_n\) (and we call \(\iota\) the identity permutation),

  • Inverse: for any \(f\in\mathrm{S}_n\text{,}\) there is unique \(f^{-1}\in\mathrm{S}_n\)  such that

    \begin{equation*} f\circ f^{-1}=f^{-1}\circ f=\iota\text{.} \end{equation*}

In Fundamentals of Pure Math (FPM), you will learn more about groups, which are sets equipped with the above structure, that is, a way of composing elements that satisfies the above properties. We have seen several examples so far:

  • real numbers equipped with \(+\text{,}\)

  • non-zero real numbers equipped with \(\cdot\) (i.e. multiplication),

  • complex numbers equipped with \(+\text{,}\)

  • non-zero complex numbers equipped with \(\cdot\text{,}\)

  • \(n\)-th root of unity equipped with \(\cdot\text{,}\)

  • \(\mathbb{Z}_n\) equipped with \(+\text{,}\)

  • \(\mathbb{Z}_n\) equipped with \(\cdot\) if \(n\) is prime (in which case every element has an inverse),
