Claim: For all \(n\in \mathbb{N}\text{,}\) the order of any cycle \(f\in S_{n}\) is at most \(2^{n}\text{.}\)

Proof: We first need to show that for all natural numbers \(n\text{,}\)

\begin{equation} n\lt 2^{n}\text{.}\tag{16.10.1} \end{equation}

(I have shown this in class, so students may also just cite the lectures for this fact.) This is true for the base case \(n=1\text{,}\) so suppose \(n\geq 1\) is an integer so that \(2^{n}>n\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} n+1\lt 2^{n}+1\lt 2^{n}+2^{n}=2^{n+1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

This proves the induction step and hence (1).

Let \(n\in\mathbb{N}\) and let \(f\in S_{n}\text{.}\) Then \(f\) can be written as a product of cycles of lengths \(r_{1},....,r_{k}\) where \(r_{i}\in \mathbb{N}\) and \(r_{1}+\cdots + r_{k}=n\text{.}\) Then the order is the least common multiple of these numbers, which is at most

\begin{equation*} r_{1}\cdots r_{k}\lt 2^{r_{1}}\cdots 2^{r_{k}}=2^{r_{1}+\cdots + r_{k}}=2^{n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
