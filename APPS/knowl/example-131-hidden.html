Let's revisit the \(9\)-puzzle and start with the one we solved earlier, that is, where we wanted to rearrange

\begin{equation*} \begin{matrix}\Box \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr1 \amp 4\amp 5 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix} \;\;\;\; \mbox{ into } \;\;\;\; \begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp \Box \cr \end{matrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

If we consider the \(\Box\) as being \(9\text{,}\) the above arrangement on the left corresponds to applying the permutation

\begin{equation*} f=\left(\begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \amp 7 \amp 8 \amp 9 \cr4 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 5 \amp 6 \amp 9 \amp 7 \amp 8 \amp 1\cr \end{matrix} \right) =(1 \; 4 \; 5 \; 6 \; 9 )(2)(3)(7)(8)=(1 \; 4 \; 5 \; 6 \; 9 ) \end{equation*}

to the grid on the right. Performing a move by moving a number into the \(\Box\) corresponds to applying a \(2\)-cycle. Let's revisit the moves we did earlier to solve this puzzle and indicate what \(2\)-cycles we are using:

\begin{equation*} \begin{matrix}\Box \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr1 \amp 4\amp 5 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix} \;\; \stackrel{(1 \; 4)}{\longrightarrow} \;\; \begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr \Box \amp 4\amp 5 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix} \;\; \stackrel{(4 \; 5)}{\longrightarrow} \;\; \begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr4 \amp \Box \amp 5 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix} \;\; \stackrel{(5 \; 6)}{\longrightarrow} \;\; \begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr4 \amp 5 \amp \Box \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix} \;\; \stackrel{(6 \; 9)}{\longrightarrow} \;\; \begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp \Box \cr \end{matrix} \end{equation*}

Thus, applying the permutation \((6 \; 9)(5 \; 6)(4 \; 5)(1 \; 4)\) returns the board to the original order, that is, it inverts the permutation \((1 \; 4 \; 5 \; 6 \; 9 )\text{.}\)

Now let's look at the following puzzle:

\begin{equation*} \begin{matrix}\Box \amp 3 \amp 2 \cr1 \amp 4\amp 5 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp 6 \cr \end{matrix} \;\;\;\; \mbox{ into } \;\;\;\; \begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \cr4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \cr7 \amp 8 \amp \Box \cr \end{matrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The permutation corresponding to this arrangement is

\begin{equation*} g=\left(\begin{matrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \amp 7 \amp 8 \amp 9 \cr4 \amp 3 \amp 2 \amp 5 \amp 6 \amp 9 \amp 7 \amp 8 \amp 1\cr \end{matrix} \right) =(1 \; 4 \; 5 \; 6 \; 9 )(2\; 3)(7)(8)=(1 \; 4 \; 5 \; 6 \; 9 )(2 \; 3) \end{equation*}

Notice that if the puzzle can be solved, that requires moving the square up or down an even number of times, and left and right an even number of times. Thus, the puzzle must take an even number of moves, and since each move is a \(2\)-cycle, that implies that the inverse of the above permutation must be even, and hence the permutation itself must also be even (this uses Lemma 7.10 (a),(d) and (e)). However, the signature of the above permutation (using Lemma 7.10 (b) and (c)) is

\begin{equation*} \mbox{sgn} \Big((1 \; 4 \; 5 \; 6 \; 9)(2 \; 3)\Big) =\mbox{sgn} \Big((1 \; 4 \; 5 \; 6 \; 9)\Big)\mbox{sgn} \Big((2 \; 3)\Big) =(-1)^{5-1}(-1)^{2-1} = -1\text{.} \end{equation*}

and so this is an odd permutation, and this gives a contradiction. Thus, the original puzzle is not solvable.
