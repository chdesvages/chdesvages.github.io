Proof

We split into two cases depending on if \(n\) is even or odd. If \(n\) is even, then \(n/2\) and \(n/2-1\) are coprime. Let \(f\) have cycle decomposition

\begin{equation*} f=(1\; 2\cdots n/2)(n/2+1 \;\; n/2+2...n-1)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then these two cycles have lengths \(n/2\) and \(n/2-1\) respectively, and the order of \(f\) is the least common multiple of these two numbers by Proposition 20.4, and since they are comprime, this must be \(\frac{n}{2}\left(\frac{n}{2}-1\right)\text{.}\)

If \(n\) is odd, then \(\frac{n-1}{2}\) and \(\frac{n+1}{2}\) are coprime. Let \(g\) have cycle decomposition

\begin{equation*} g=(1\; 2\cdots (n-1)/2)((n+1)/2 ... n) \end{equation*}

then the lengths of the cycles in this decomposition are \(\frac{n-1}{2}\) and \(\frac{n+1}{2}\text{,}\) and so the order of the cycle is the least common multiple of these numbers by Proposition 20.4 which (because they are coprime) is just their product: \(\frac{n-1}{2}\frac{n+1}{2}= \frac{n^2-1}{4}\text{.}\)

Taking the minimum of this and \(\frac{n}{2}\left(\frac{n}{2}-1\right)\text{,}\) we see that \(L(n)\geq \frac{n}{2}\left(\frac{n}{2}-1\right)\) for all \(n\in\mathbb{N}\text{.}\)
