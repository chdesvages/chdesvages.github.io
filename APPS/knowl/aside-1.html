A note about set theory.

Properly speaking, the term “set” is a primitive notion. Both it and the symbol “\(\in\)” are left undefined, and mathematicians rely on a collection of axioms to reflect their intuitions on how these objects ought to work.

Interestingly, there are lots of different systems of axioms for sets out there, which correspond to different intuitions about how these notions ought to behave. The study of these different axiom systems and their interrelations is part of set theory. Set theory is closely tied to logic.

For now, we are mostly going to stick to a relatively informal discussion of the most widely agreed-upon axioms. These are the Zermelo–Fraenkel axioms of set theory. Most, mathematicians also include one further axiom: the Axiom of Choice.

What we will do here is what is often called naive set theory. We won't worry excessively about formalizing our sets; instead, we'll focus on how to be responsible end-users of sets.
