Suppose \(x_k\) appears in each of the sets \(A_{k_{1}}, \dots ,A_{k_{m}}\) for some \(1\leq k_1\lt k_2\lt \cdots \lt k_m\leq n\text{,}\) and none of the others. This means \(x_k\) appears in exactly \(m\) sets, so its contribution to \(N_{1}\) is exactly \(m\text{.}\) It also appears in \({m\choose 2}\) many pairs \(A_{k_{i}}\cap A_{k_{j}}\) with \(1\leq i\lt j\leq m\text{,}\) so it contributes \({m\choose 2}\) to \(N_2\text{.}\) Similarly, \(x\) contributes \({m\choose k}\) to \(N_k\) for each \(k\leq n\) (and recall that \({m\choose k}=0\) for \(k>m\)). Thus, by the Binomial Theorem, the total contribution of \(x\) to the right side of (15.6.1) is

\begin{equation*} \sum_{k=1}^{m} {m\choose k}(-1)^{k+1} =1 + \sum_{k=0}^{n} {m\choose k}(-1)^{k+1} =1 -\sum_{k=0}^{n} {m\choose k}(-1)^{k} =1-(1-1)^{m}=1\text{.} \end{equation*}
