For example, let \(A = \{1,2,3,4,5,6\}\text{.}\) Then \(\{\{1,2,3\},\{4,5\},\{6\}\}\) is a partition of \(A\) into sets of sizes \(3\text{,}\) \(2\) and \(1\) respectively. Another one is \(\{\{1,4,6\},\{3,5\},\{2\}\}\text{.}\) How many such partitions are there? One way to look at this is as follows. First we work out the number of ways to select \(3\) members of \(A\) for our first set: there are \({6 \choose 3}\) such ways. Each such choice leaves us with a set of \(3\) remaining members of \(A\text{,}\) from which we choose \(2\) for our second set, and there are \(3 \choose 2\) ways to do this. Finally there is exactly one choice for the last set. So altogether there are

\begin{equation*} {6 \choose 3} \times {3 \choose 2} \times 1= \frac{6!}{3! 3!} \times \frac{3!}{2! 1!} \times \frac{1!}{1!} = \frac{6!}{3! 2! 1!} \end{equation*}

ways to form such a partition, by the Multiplication Principle.
