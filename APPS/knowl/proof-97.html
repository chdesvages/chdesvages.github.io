Proof

Let \(A=A_{1}\cup\cdots \cup A_{n}\text{,}\) and list the elements as \(A=\{x_{1}, \dots ,x_{M}\}\) where \(M=|A|\text{.}\) Notice that when we compute \(N_1=|A_{1}|+\cdots + |A_{n}|\text{,}\) this is the same as computing for each \(x_k\) the number of sets \(A_i\) that \(x_k\) appears in, and then summing over \(k\text{.}\) Similarly, \(c_2\) is the same as adding together over all \(x_k\) the number of times \(x_k\) appears in some pair \(A_i\cap A_j\text{,}\) and so on. Thus, the sum on the right of (15.6.1) is the same as computing the sum over each \(x_k\text{,}\) how many times \(x_k\) appears in one set \(A_i\) minus how many times it appears in the intersection of two sets, and so on, and then adding over all \(x_k\in A_{1}\cup\cdots \cup A_{n}\text{.}\) What we will do is show that for each \(k\text{,}\) this number is \(1\text{.}\)

Suppose \(x_k\) appears in each of the sets \(A_{k_{1}}, \dots ,A_{k_{m}}\) for some \(1\leq k_1\lt k_2\lt \cdots \lt k_m\leq n\text{,}\) and none of the others. This means \(x_k\) appears in exactly \(m\) sets, so its contribution to \(N_{1}\) is exactly \(m\text{.}\) It also appears in \({m\choose 2}\) many pairs \(A_{k_{i}}\cap A_{k_{j}}\) with \(1\leq i\lt j\leq m\text{,}\) so it contributes \({m\choose 2}\) to \(N_2\text{.}\) Similarly, \(x\) contributes \({m\choose k}\) to \(N_k\) for each \(k\leq n\) (and recall that \({m\choose k}=0\) for \(k>m\)). Thus, by the Binomial Theorem, the total contribution of \(x\) to the right side of (15.6.1) is

\begin{equation*} \sum_{k=1}^{m} {m\choose k}(-1)^{k+1} =1 + \sum_{k=0}^{n} {m\choose k}(-1)^{k+1} =1 -\sum_{k=0}^{n} {m\choose k}(-1)^{k} =1-(1-1)^{m}=1\text{.} \end{equation*}
