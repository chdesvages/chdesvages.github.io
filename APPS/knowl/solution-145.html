Solution [STRUCT].0.1.

Write

\begin{equation*} 4=xy+2x+3y =(x+3)(y+2)-6 \end{equation*}

So now we have

\begin{equation*} (x+3)(y+2)=10=2\cdot 5=10\cdot 1 \end{equation*}

and so the only way this can happen is if either

  • \(y+2=2\text{,}\) \(x+3=5\) (which implies \(y=0\) and \(x=2\))

  • \(y+2=-2\text{,}\) \(x+3=-5\) (so \(y=-4\) and \(x=-8\))

  • \(y+2=5\text{,}\) \(x+3=2\) (so \(y=3\) and \(x=-1\))

  • \(y+2=-5\text{,}\) \(x+3=-2\) (so \(y=-7)\) and \(x=-5\text{.}\)

  • \(y+2=10\) and \(x+3=1\) (so \((x,y)=(-2,8))\)

  • \(y+2=-10\) and \(x+3=-1\) (so \((x,y)=(-4,-12))\)

  • \(y+2=1\) and \(x+3=10\) (so \((x,y)=(7,-1)\))

  • \(y+2=-1\) and \(x+3=-10\) (so \((x,y)=(-13,-3)\))

So the above pairs of \((x,y)\) are the only solutions.
