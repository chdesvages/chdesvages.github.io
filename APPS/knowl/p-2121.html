Note that if \(f\) is a cycle of length \(r\) in \(\mathrm{S}_n\text{,}\) then the powers follow the following pattern:

\begin{equation*} \ldots f^{-1}=f^{r-1},\ \underbrace{\iota=f^{0},\ f^{1},\ f^{2},\ f^{3},\ldots,\ f^{r-1}},\ f^{r}=\iota,\ f^{r+1}=f, \ldots \end{equation*}

where the permutations \(f^{0},f^{1},...,f^{r-1}\) are all distinct. This is because, if \(f=(a_{1}\; \cdots \; a_{r})\) and \(0\leq k \lt r\text{,}\) then \(f^{k}(a_{1})=a_{1+k}\text{,}\) which ranges through \(a_{1},a_{2},...,a_{r}\) as \(k=0,1,...,r-1\) and these are all distinct numbers. Moreover, \(f^{r}(a_{1})=f\circ f^{r-1}(a_{1})=f(a_{r})=a_{1}\text{,}\) and recalling (16.6.1), we can also show that \(f^{r}(a_{i})=a_{i}\) for \(i=1,...,r\text{.}\) Since \(f(k)=k\) for all \(k\not\in \{a_{1},...a_{r}\}\) anyway, this means \(f^{r}(k)\) for those \(k\text{,}\) and thus \(f^{r}=\iota\text{.}\) We have thus shown the following.
