Now suppose we know that claim b holds for all \(z\) with \(|z|^2\leq n\) for some integer \(n\text{.}\) Let \(z\) now be an element with \(|z|^2=n+1\text{.}\) If it is not primo, then there is a way of writing z as \(xy\) where neither x or y are equal to plus or minus z, so in particular, neither of them are \(\pm 1\) either. By part (a), since neither of them are 1, then \(|x|,|y|>1\text{,}\) and \(|z|=|xy|=|x||y|\text{,}\) so \(1\lt |x|^2=|z|^2/|y|^2\lt |z|^2=n+1\text{.}\) Thus, \(x\) can be factored into a product of primos by the induction hypothesis, and y can be similarly. This proves the induction step.
